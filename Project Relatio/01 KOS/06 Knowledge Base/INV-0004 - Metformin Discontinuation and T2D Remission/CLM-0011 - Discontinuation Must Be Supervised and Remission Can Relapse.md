# CLM-0011

# Discontinuation Must Be Supervised and Remission Can Relapse

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> Stopping metformin or any glucose-lowering medication must be a **physician-supervised, monitored** process — never a unilateral one — and even genuine remission is a state **requiring ongoing observation because it frequently relapses over time**. Achieving remission does not make diabetes "cured."

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Normative** (what should be done: supervise and monitor) **+ descriptive** (remission relapses at measurable rates). Normative claims require an ethical/clinical value framework and consequences (KOS-0003 §3.5); here the framework is patient-safety / harm-avoidance.

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Traditional/authoritative** (consensus guidance) + **empirical** (relapse rates).
- **SRC-0010 (ADA/EASD consensus):** remission is a state that "**requires continued observation because hyperglycaemia frequently recurs**"; recommends HbA1c monitoring **at least yearly** plus continued complication screening. The very definition requires being off medication for ≥3 months *before* remission is even declared.
- **SRC-0006 (DiRECT):** remission fell from **46% (1 yr) → 36% (2 yr) → ~10% (5 yr)**, tracking weight regain — direct empirical evidence that remission **relapses** in a majority over 5 years.
- **Deprescribing practice (verified directionally this session):** metformin is typically the **last** glucose-lowering drug withdrawn (and is sometimes retained for cardiovascular/renal benefit); abrupt cessation can raise blood glucose; monitoring (e.g. HbA1c at ~3 months) is standard.

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- **Reliability: 5** — governing consensus plus a randomised trial's own long-term data.
- **Relevance: 5** — directly answers the "can she come off metformin?" safety question.
- **Independence: 5** — consensus (definitional) and DiRECT (empirical relapse) are independent and agree.
- **Quality: 4** — high; the specific deprescribing-sequence points are from guidance/summaries rather than a single RCT.
- **Limitations:** Establishes that supervision and monitoring are the standard and that relapse is common; it does **not** quantify relapse risk for any individual, nor prescribe a specific tapering protocol.

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- Evidence strength: **High.**
- Consensus strength: **High** — universal in guidelines: de-prescribe under supervision; monitor; remission is not cure.
- Relationship: strongly and uncontroversially supported.

---

# Source Evaluation
SRC-0010: highest-authority definitional consensus. SRC-0006: high-authority RCT with candidly-reported relapse. Convergent.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Normative/ethical:** avoiding foreseeable harm (rebound hyperglycaemia, undetected relapse) outweighs the convenience of stopping medication.
- **Methodological:** trial-population relapse rates are indicative (not exact) for individuals.

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Deductive** on the safety norm (if remission requires being off medication for ≥3 months *and* hyperglycaemia frequently recurs, then monitoring and supervision are necessary to catch relapse safely) + **inductive** on relapse frequency (DiRECT trajectory). Risks checked: **motivated reasoning** — the "come off your drugs" narrative is explicitly countered here; **emotional reasoning** — the desire to stop medication does not change the risk profile.

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8)
**Level 4 — High.** The safety norm and the relapse phenomenon are both strongly supported and consensual. Held below Level 5 only because relapse *rates* are population-level and individual de-prescribing protocols vary. This is the investigation's **safety-anchoring claim** and constrains the overall finding.

---

# Limitations
- Does not specify an individual's tapering schedule or monitoring interval — that is a clinical decision.
- Relapse frequency is context-dependent (degree and maintenance of weight loss).

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **Once in remission, medication and monitoring can simply stop.** *Rejected* — contradicts the consensus (yearly monitoring; hyperglycaemia recurs) and DiRECT's relapse trajectory.
2. **Metformin can be stopped abruptly if HbA1c is normal.** *Rejected* — abrupt cessation can raise glucose; supervision and follow-up testing are standard.
