# SRC-0073

# Skjærvø — Avestan Transmission and the Dating Problem

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Prods Oktor Skjærvø (Emeritus Professor of Iranian Studies, Harvard), on the composition, oral transmission, and written fixation of the Avesta — the canonical reference for this KB is **"The Zoroastrian Oral Tradition as Reflected in the Texts," in A. Cantera (ed.), *The Transmission of the Avesta* (Iranica 20; Wiesbaden: Harrassowitz, 2012), pp. 3–48**, together with "The Gathas, a Forgotten Masterpiece" (*Wiley Blackwell Companion to Zoroastrianism*, 2015) and his introductory studies to Old Avestan. **In this KB it is the primary anchor for the dating/transmission problem (CLM-0056):** the Avesta was composed across the 2nd–early 1st millennium BCE, transmitted *orally* by priests for roughly a millennium-plus, and only fixed in writing under the Sasanians (c. 6th–7th c. CE) in a script invented for the purpose. The linguistic layers (Old Avestan / Gathic vs Younger Avestan) are distinguished by comparative philology, and even those relative dates are reconstruction.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction) — it establishes, by philological reconstruction, precisely *why* the Zoroastrian primary evidence cannot be dated by ordinary manuscript witness.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very high — a leading authority on Avestan language and its transmission.
- **Transparency:** High — explicit about what is reconstruction and about the length and effects of oral transmission (diascevast standardization; later scribal reshaping).
- **Independence:** High — this is technical philology, not a position in the influence dispute; it constrains *all* parties.
- **Intent:** To reconstruct the history of the Avestan text and its languages.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It establishes the *transmission and dating framework*, not any doctrine's substantive history — it tells us how securely (not) a doctrine can be dated, not whether it influenced Judaism.
- The relative dating of the layers is itself reconstruction; absolute dates for Zoroaster/the Gathas remain contested (cf. discrimination-sample #1–#2).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The Avesta was orally transmitted for ~a millennium and written down only under the Sasanians (6th–7th c. CE); no early manuscript witness exists.
- Old Avestan (Gathic) and Younger Avestan are distinguished by comparative philology; their dates are reconstructions, not attested.
- Editorial standardization during oral transmission, plus later scribal reshaping, complicate reading late-attested text as verbatim-archaic.
