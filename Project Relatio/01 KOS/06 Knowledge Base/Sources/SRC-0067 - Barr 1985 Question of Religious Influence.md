# SRC-0067

# Barr 1985 — The Question of Religious Influence: Zoroastrianism, Judaism, and Christianity

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
James Barr, "The Question of Religious Influence: The Case of Zoroastrianism, Judaism, and Christianity," *Journal of the American Academy of Religion* LIII/2 (June 1985): 201–236 (DOI 10.1093/jaarel/LIII.2.201). The classic methodological caution in the field: it observes that it is customary to connect later-Old-Testament and post-biblical Jewish phenomena (angels, dualism, eschatology, bodily resurrection) with Iranian influence, but that biblical/Jewish scholarship has remained "aloof" from the Iranological evidence — and that the influence thesis is routinely asserted on parallel alone, without controlling for the dating and transmission problems on the Iranian side. **In this KB it is the skeptical / methodological anchor** against which every directional-influence claim is tested.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction/critical argument) — it is a 20th-century critical essay, not a witness to the events. Its value is as a *discipline on inference*, not as attestation.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — Barr was a leading Hebrew Bible / Semitics scholar (Oxford); the article is the standard citation for the cautious position.
- **Transparency:** High — argues explicitly from the asymmetry of evidence and names the methodological gaps (chronology, philological competence, direction of borrowing).
- **Independence:** High — no confessional or perennialist program; the argument cuts against *both* an easy "all borrowed" and an easy "all internal" story by insisting the evidence be controlled.
- **Intent:** To puncture uncritical influence-assertion and to specify what would have to be shown for an influence claim to hold.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It does **not** establish that there was *no* influence — Barr's claim is that influence is *not demonstrated* by the usual arguments, not that it is disproven. Using it as a blanket denial would misread it.
- It is a 1985 essay; some Iranological dating scholarship it responds to has since been refined (Skjærvø, de Jong, Hultgård). Its methodological point survives; its specific state-of-the-field claims are dated.
- It adjudicates method, not any single doctrine's history on its own.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The influence thesis is asserted on *parallel* far more than on *demonstrated chronological priority + transmission channel*.
- The Iranian primary evidence is late-attested and orally transmitted; the burden of proof for directional influence is therefore heavier than the parallel-spotting literature acknowledges.
