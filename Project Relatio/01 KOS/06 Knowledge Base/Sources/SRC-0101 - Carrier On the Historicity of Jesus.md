# SRC-0101

# Carrier — On the Historicity of Jesus (2014)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Richard Carrier, *On the Historicity of Jesus: Why We Might Have Reason for Doubt* (Sheffield Phoenix Press, 2014). The most rigorous scholarly statement of the **mythicist** position — that the Jesus of the earliest sources may be best explained as a celestial/mythic figure later historicized — argued via a Bayesian framework. In this KB it is included to **steelman the mythicist pole at its strongest** (OPS-0003 Non-Negotiable 6), so that the field consensus (SRC-0100) is represented as *winning an argument*, not as an unexamined default. It is an academic-press (peer-reviewed) monograph, which is why it is used rather than popular mythicist writing.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction) — a critical-historical argument, minority position.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Mixed/contested — Carrier holds a Columbia PhD in ancient history and the book is from an academic press, but the thesis is a **fringe minority** rejected by virtually all specialists; its authority is as the best-argued statement of that minority, not as a consensus position.
- **Transparency:** High — the Bayesian method and priors are laid out explicitly and are open to challenge (and have been challenged in detail).
- **Independence:** High — no confessional stake; if anything an anti-confessional motivation, which must itself be named as a possible bias.
- **Intent:** To persuade toward doubt about historicity — strongly argumentative; the motivated-reasoning guard cuts both ways (an anti-religious prior is as much a bias to check as a confessional one).

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It represents a **minority/fringe** view; its inclusion establishes that the historicity question has been *argued*, not that mythicism is correct — the consensus (SRC-0100) rejects it.
- Its Bayesian reconstruction depends heavily on contested priors and on a particular reading of Paul (a celestial-Christ reading) that most specialists reject; the KB records it as a steelmanned alternative, not as an accepted result.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The strongest form of the mythicist case: that Paul's letters can be read as referring to a revealed/celestial Christ rather than a recently-deceased Galilean, and that the Gospels are later historicizing narrative.
- Used here **only** to test whether the consensus survives the strongest challenge — it does, on the majority evaluation (SRC-0100, SRC-0097, SRC-0099).
