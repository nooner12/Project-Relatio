# SRC-0005

# Slingerland — *Effortless Action* (2003)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification

Edward Slingerland, *Effortless Action: Wu-wei as Conceptual Metaphor and Spiritual Ideal in Early China* (Oxford University Press, 2003). A scholarly monograph treating *wu wei* as a conceptual metaphor and tracing it across early Chinese thought — including Confucian as well as Daoist texts.

---

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)

- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed academic monograph by a specialist in early Chinese thought and cognitive science of religion.
- **Transparency:** High — explicit method (conceptual-metaphor analysis), extensive textual citation.
- **Independence:** A secondary/interpretive source; independent scholarly analysis of the primary texts (SRC-0002).
- **Intent:** Scholarly explanation and interpretation.

---

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)

- One influential interpretation among several; the conceptual-metaphor framing is itself a scholarly choice.
- Read here in summary; specific textual arguments are not reproduced in full.

---

# 4. Key Content Used

- *Wu wei* is best understood as **"effortless action,"** not literal inaction.
- The ideal is **not exclusively Daoist** — a "wu wei"-like effortless ideal appears across early Chinese schools (Confucius, Mencius, Xunzi, Zhuangzi), each cultivating it differently.
- The "paradox of wu wei": how does one *try* to *not try*? — a genuine tension the traditions grapple with.
