# SRC-0031

# Mantzari et al. 2015 — Personal Financial Incentives for Changing Habitual Health Behaviours (Meta-Analysis)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Systematic review and meta-analysis of **personal financial incentives** for changing habitual health behaviours (smoking cessation, physical activity, healthier eating, weight loss, screening/vaccination, medication adherence): Mantzari et al. (2015), *Preventive Medicine* 75:75–85. It is the key evidence for the durability limit of external-contingency interventions.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed meta-analysis from the Cambridge Behaviour and Health Research Unit (Marteau group), including a leading meta-analytic methodologist (Higgins).
- **Transparency:** High — timepoint-stratified pooled odds ratios and confidence intervals reported.
- **Independence:** High — academic, public-health oriented.
- **Intent:** Inform.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Heterogeneous behaviours pooled; effects vary by behaviour (e.g. smoking cessation somewhat more persistent).
- It establishes an **absence of sustained effect at the timepoints studied**, which is uncertainty about long-run effect, argued by the authors as "new habits do not appear to be formed."
- Does not test incentives *combined with* habit/self-regulation support, which could in principle alter durability.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Incentives increased health-behaviour change during and shortly after the incentive period — e.g. **OR ≈ 1.53 (95% CI 1.05–2.23)** for changes sustained to 18 months from baseline, and **OR ≈ 2.11 (95% CI 1.21–3.67)** for changes sustained to >2–3 months post-removal (but not thereafter). *(Verified at authoring and **re-verified 2026-07-11** (remediation pass) via PMC-4728181 full text — all three odds ratios and confidence intervals confirmed exactly.)*
- **Effects were not sustained beyond ~3 months after incentive removal**; the authors concluded **"new habits do not appear to be formed."** *(Verified and re-verified this session.)*
- A larger effect among **highly-deprived** recipients (OR ≈ 2.17, 95% CI 1.22–3.85), but only at >6–12 months from baseline; suggests possible equity benefit, though this too was time-limited. *(CI re-verified exactly 2026-07-11.)*

- `supports` CLM-0026 (incentives work while in place but effects fade after removal).
