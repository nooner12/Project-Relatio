# SRC-0121

# Zaehner 1955 — Zurvan: A Zoroastrian Dilemma

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
R. C. Zaehner, *Zurvan: A Zoroastrian Dilemma* (1955).

The landmark study of Zurvanism, which collects and translates the scattered source material — much of it, necessarily, outsider testimony such as Eznik (SRC-0120) — and argues for Zurvanism as a major current within Sasanian Zoroastrianism.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High as a landmark and as a **source collection**; more qualified as an interpretation. Zaehner was a significant scholar of Zoroastrianism, and the book remains the standard point of departure for the subject.
- **Transparency:** High. It presents the texts it argues from, in translation, so a reader can assess the reconstruction against its evidence rather than taking the conclusions on trust. This is the feature that has allowed later scholarship to contest it productively.
- **Independence:** Independent scholarly work, external to the tradition — though working almost entirely from external and hostile witnesses, which bounds what independence achieves.
- **Intent:** Reconstructive — to establish Zurvanism as a substantial religious phenomenon and to make sense of its relation to Zoroastrian orthodoxy.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **The maximalist reconstruction is contested.** Zaehner's account of Zurvanism as a dominant or quasi-orthodox Sasanian current has been substantially qualified by later scholarship (see SRC-0122). It should be cited as an influential position in a continuing dispute, not as the established picture.
- The **evidential base is thin and mostly hostile** — there is no native Zoroastrian primary text attesting a Zurvanite sect (SRC-0120 §3). No reconstruction, however careful, can be more secure than that base allows, and a systematic account built from unsystematic outsider notices risks importing coherence the phenomenon may not have had.
- Published **1955**. Its enduring value now lies substantially in the **collected and translated material** rather than in the interpretive frame; those two uses should be weighted differently.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The collected and translated source material on Zurvanism, including the Armenian and other outsider testimonies.
- The reconstruction of Zurvanism as a distinct theological current within Sasanian Zoroastrianism.
