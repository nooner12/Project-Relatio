# SRC-0033

# Ashford, Edmunds & French 2010 — Changing Self-Efficacy to Promote Physical Activity (Systematic Review with Meta-Analysis)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Systematic review with meta-analysis of interventions designed to change **self-efficacy** for lifestyle and recreational physical activity, and of which behaviour-change techniques do so: Ashford, Edmunds & French (2010), *British Journal of Health Psychology* 15(2):265–288. It is the catalogued evidence base for the **self-efficacy** leg of CLM-0020 (self-efficacy as a modifiable determinant of physical-activity behaviour).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed meta-analysis in a leading health-psychology journal; heavily cited.
- **Transparency:** High — technique moderators and effect directions reported.
- **Independence:** High — academic; not authored by the construct's originator (Bandura), so lower theoretical-investment than the SDT source in the same claim.
- **Intent:** Inform.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Domain is **physical activity**; extension to diet, meditation, sleep is an inference.
- It concerns **changing self-efficacy and its association with activity**, largely at initiation / short-to-medium term; it does **not** cleanly establish that self-efficacy drives *long-term maintenance* specifically.
- Some technique effects were counter-intuitive (e.g. persuasion and graded mastery associated with *lower* measured self-efficacy), so it is not a simple "raise self-efficacy however you like" prescription.
- It complements, but does not by itself prove, the causal/durability step in CLM-0020.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Increasing self-efficacy is associated with increased physical activity; self-efficacy is a **modifiable determinant** of activity behaviour. *(Verified this session via the Wiley/BJHP record and independent bibliographic listings; abstract-level.)*
- The most effective technique for raising self-efficacy was **vicarious experience** (e.g. modelling), whereas persuasion, graded mastery, and barrier identification were associated with *lower* self-efficacy — i.e. technique choice matters. *(Verified this session, abstract-level.)*

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0020 (self-efficacy is a genuine, modifiable predictor of physical-activity behaviour — the catalogued source for the self-efficacy leg).
