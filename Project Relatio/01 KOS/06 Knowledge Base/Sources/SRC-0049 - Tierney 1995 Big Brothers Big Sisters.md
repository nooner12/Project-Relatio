# SRC-0049

# Tierney, Grossman & Resch 1995 — Making a Difference: BBBS Impact Study

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Tierney, J. P., Grossman, J. B., & Resch, N. L. (1995), *Making a Difference: An Impact Study of Big Brothers/Big Sisters*, Philadelphia: Public/Private Ventures. A **randomized controlled trial**: 959 youth aged 10–16 who applied to 8 BB/BSA agencies (1992–93) were randomly assigned to a match (treatment) or an 18-month waiting list (control). The landmark experimental corroboration for domain 4. Verified this session via the P/PV full report (IssueLab), ERIC (ED390973), and OJP.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High for design — a rare true RCT of community mentoring, widely cited as the field's benchmark.
- **Transparency:** High — random assignment, sample, follow-up window, and outcome set reported.
- **Independence:** Moderate — conducted by Public/Private Ventures (an intermediary evaluating a partner program), a potential mild conflict, offset by the experimental design and null/small results reported honestly.
- **Intent:** Evaluate BBBS impact rigorously for the field.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Effects were **statistically significant but small in magnitude** and concentrated at **18-month** follow-up; long-term durability is not established here.
- A commonly cited **standardized mean difference of ≈0.06** comes from later re-analysis/summary (e.g., Blueprints), **not** the original report's headline; treat the 0.06 as a secondary-source magnitude, not verified in the primary.
- It evaluates **one program model** (community friendship mentoring), **not** skills-based, heritage, or nature programming; transfer to those is analogical.
- **Funding:** P/PV / foundation-funded evaluation (not itemised this session).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Random assignment of 959 youth; **18-month** follow-up.
- Favourable effects reported by youth on: **initiation of drug/alcohol use** (reduced), **school attendance/grades**, **hitting/fighting** (reduced), and **quality of parental and peer relationships** — all **modest**.
- Benefits observed across race/ethnicity and gender subgroups.
