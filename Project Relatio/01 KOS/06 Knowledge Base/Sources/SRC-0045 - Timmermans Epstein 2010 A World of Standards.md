# SRC-0045

# Timmermans & Epstein 2010 — A World of Standards but not a Standard World

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Timmermans, S., & Epstein, S. (2010), "A World of Standards but not a Standard World: Toward a Sociology of Standards and Standardization," *Annual Review of Sociology* 36:69–89. A review of the sociology of standards and standardization. Catalogued as the **adversarial anchor** for CLM-0033's steelman: standardization is a ubiquitous, valuable feature of modern life whose function is often to impose **uniform equivalence across contexts** — a partial counterweight to the "locally fitted / congruent / participatory" prescription.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed *Annual Review* synthesis by two recognised sociologists; heavily cited.
- **Transparency:** High — an explicit literature review with a stated organizing scheme (emergence/institutionalization; making standards work; resistance; outcomes).
- **Independence:** High — academic; deliberately even-handed rather than pro- or anti-standardization.
- **Intent:** Inform / synthesise a field.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It is a **balanced review**, and does **not** argue that "rigid, non-revisable standards are superior." It is used here only to establish that **non-local, uniform, often non-participatory standardization is pervasive and functional** — the reality that pressures CLM-0033's participatory/congruence prescription. Overreading it as a pro-rigidity argument would misrepresent it.
- It is sociological/descriptive; it does not supply effect sizes or a decision rule for when rigidity beats revisability.
- Its evidence is about standards broadly (measurement, safety, classification), not knowledge-architecture governance specifically.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Standards "render the world equivalent across cultures, time, and geography" — their value frequently lies in **uniformity**, which by design **overrides local variation** rather than fitting it.
- The review treats **the difficulty of making standards work** and **resistance to standardization** as central themes — i.e., standardization is contested and imperfect, but also indispensable and ubiquitous.
- Used here to show that the "congruence + participatory revision" resolution of CLM-0033 has a **real counterweight**: much valuable formal machinery is deliberately uniform and non-participatory.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `contrasts_with` CLM-0033 (the adversarial anchor for the "uniform/non-participatory standardization is also valuable" steelman).
