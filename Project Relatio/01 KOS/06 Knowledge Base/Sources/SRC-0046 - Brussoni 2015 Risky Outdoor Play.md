# SRC-0046

# Brussoni et al. 2015 — Risky Outdoor Play and Health in Children (Systematic Review)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Brussoni, M., et al. (2015), "What is the Relationship between Risky Outdoor Play and Health in Children? A Systematic Review," *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health* 12(6):6423–6454. A systematic review of **21 studies** using the GRADE framework, examining three categories of risky play and risk-supportive environments against child health/behaviour outcomes. It is the primary anchor for domain 2 (risky play). Verified this session via PubMed (PMID 26062038), the PMC full text, and MDPI.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed; led by a recognised injury-prevention/child-development group (UBC); uses a formal evidence-grading framework (GRADE).
- **Transparency:** High — inclusion criteria, the 21 studies, and quality appraisal are reported; the authors state where evidence is weak.
- **Independence:** Moderate–High — academic; but the topic area carries an advocacy current (promoting risky play), so the review's own quality caveats matter.
- **Intent:** Synthesise evidence and (explicitly) support promotion of risky outdoor play; the advocacy intent is stated, which aids appraisal.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- The reviewed evidence is **mostly observational / cross-sectional**; the review reports **associations**, not established causal effects, and the authors call for higher-quality studies.
- Strongest, most consistent findings are for **physical activity** and **social health**; effects on **self-regulation, resilience, and anxiety** are far more weakly evidenced — the field's enthusiasm for these outcomes exceeds the graded evidence.
- On **injury**, the review supports that risky play is associated with predominantly *minor* injuries and does not establish an increase in serious injury — but it does not license "risky play is safe" as an unconditional claim.
- **Funding:** not independently confirmed this session; Brussoni's broader program has received Canadian public-health/child-injury research funding (e.g., CIHR-adjacent bodies). State as **not verified this session**.
- Says nothing about **heritage-skills / craft** content specifically, nor about mentored program delivery.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Overall **positive** associations between risky outdoor play and health indicators, **most consistently physical activity**, plus social health/behaviour; some evidence for reduced aggression.
- Play where children "can disappear / get lost" (exploration): 7 of 8 relevant papers found increases in physical activity and social health.
- GRADE appraisal: evidence quality **low–moderate**; call for "good quality" studies.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0035 (risky-play outcomes), with the split grade the claim carries.
