# Ehrman — Forgery and Counterforgery (2013)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Bart D. Ehrman, *Forgery and Counterforgery: The Use of Literary Deceit in Early Christian Polemics* (Oxford University Press, 2013). Ehrman's scholarly monograph (as distinct from his trade book *Forged*) on pseudonymity and pseudepigraphy in early Christian literature. In this KB it supports the **authorship gradient**: the argument that the disputed Pauline letters, and the Pastorals in particular, are pseudonymous ("written in Paul's name by someone else"), and the technical apparatus (stylometry, anachronism, theological development) behind those judgments.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction) — a critical-historical monograph on authorship.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — a peer-reviewed academic monograph (OUP) in Ehrman's specialist area.
- **Transparency:** High — argues from ancient conceptions of authorship and from internal-evidence criteria; distinguishes secure from disputed cases.
- **Independence:** High — non-confessional; the pseudonymity thesis is broadly held across the critical field (Brown, Schnelle, etc.).
- **Intent:** To argue a scholarly thesis (that early Christians did practice deceptive pseudepigraphy) — assertive, but by academic argument.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Pseudonymity judgments are **H-e reconstructions** from internal evidence (style, vocabulary, theology, presupposed church structures); they carry graded, not certain, confidence — the disputed Deutero-Paulines especially remain genuinely divided.
- Ehrman argues the strong "deceit" framing; a minority of scholars read some cases as accepted school-convention rather than deception — a live interpretive dispute the claim must carry.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The Pastorals (1–2 Timothy, Titus) are, for most critical scholars, pseudonymous — presupposing developed church offices and using non-Pauline vocabulary/style.
- Ephesians, Colossians, and 2 Thessalonians are disputed on stylistic and theological grounds; the seven undisputed letters are the fixed baseline against which the others are measured.
