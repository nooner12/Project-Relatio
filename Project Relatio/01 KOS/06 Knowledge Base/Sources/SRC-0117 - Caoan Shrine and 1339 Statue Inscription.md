# SRC-0117

# Caoan Shrine and 1339 Statue Inscription

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
The Cao'an shrine at Jinjiang, Fujian province, China — a stone structure housing a relief statue of Mani as the "Buddha of Light," with an associated donor inscription **dated 1339 CE** (Yuan period). It is the surviving Manichaean shrine, and the material evidence for the persistence of Manichaean worship in southeastern China into the Yuan–Ming era.

Catalogued as a **primary, material, dated** source.

**No object/edition split made.** The source base names no separate scholarly edition of the inscription. Lieu 1992 (SRC-0116) is cited alongside it but is a **secondary study, not an edition**, and is catalogued as its own record; no edition has been invented to force a split.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High for what it directly attests. Material, in situ, and **explicitly dated** — a dated inscription is among the strongest forms of attestation available for a question of this kind, since it fixes a point in time without depending on any transmitted text.
- **Transparency:** High as an artifact. The shrine and statue are extant, accessible, and published; the inscription can be examined directly rather than through a transmission chain.
- **Independence:** In-group — this is a devotional artifact made by and for the community. It is not independent testimony about Manichaeism. But it is **material rather than literary**, which changes the failure modes: a shrine is not subject to scribal alteration, hagiographic shaping, or tendentious recopying in the way a text is.
- **Intent:** Devotional and donative — to commission and dedicate a cult image. Not documentary, and not addressed to posterity, which is part of why it is comparatively unguarded evidence.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It attests the **shrine's existence and a dated act of patronage**. Whether the cult practised there in 1339 was Manichaean *in doctrine and self-understanding* rests on a further interpretive step — the identification of the figure as Mani depends on iconographic and textual argument, and the site shows clear Buddhist assimilation.
- **A single site does not establish extent.** That Manichaean worship persisted at Cao'an says nothing reliable about how widespread it remained in Fujian or elsewhere; one survival is compatible with near-total disappearance.
- It bears on **persistence in China**, and carries no weight for Mani's own life, the Iranian material, or the Roman-empire history.
- The dated inscription fixes **1339**; it does not establish continuity of practice before or after that date.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The 1339 dated donor inscription associated with the Mani "Buddha of Light" statue.
- The physical survival of the shrine and its relief image.
