# SRC-0099

# Non-Christian Primary Testimonia — Josephus, Tacitus, Pliny

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
The three principal **non-Christian primary notices** bearing on Jesus and the earliest movement, treated here as one primary-source record:
- **Josephus, *Antiquities of the Jews* 18.63–64** (the *Testimonium Flavianum*) and **20.200** (the incidental notice of "James, the brother of Jesus who was called Christ"), written c. 93–94 CE.
- **Tacitus, *Annals* 15.44** (on the Neronian persecution of 64 CE; "Christus... executed under Pontius Pilate in the reign of Tiberius"), written c. 116–117 CE.
- **Pliny the Younger, *Epistles* 10.96** (to Trajan; Christians in Bithynia "singing a hymn to Christ as to a god"), written c. 111–113 CE.
In this KB these are the **external, non-Christian attestation** evaluated in CLM-0073, with SRC-0098 (Meier) as the scholarly evaluation of the Josephan portion.

**H-band of this record:** **H-c** (near-contemporary secondary accounts) — written roughly two to three generations after the events, by authors who are not eyewitnesses and, for Tacitus and Pliny, likely relaying information about (not from) the movement.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High as historical sources — Josephus (a Jewish historian), Tacitus (a senatorial historian), and Pliny (an imperial governor) are among the best-regarded ancient writers; but none is a witness to Jesus.
- **Transparency:** Mixed — Tacitus writes with administrative specificity (Pilate, Tiberius); Pliny reports his own interrogations; Josephus's TF is textually compromised by Christian interpolation.
- **Independence:** Partial and disputed — Tacitus and Pliny may relay what Christians (or Roman records about Christians) reported, so their *independence from Christian tradition* is limited; *Ant.* 20.200 is the most plausibly independent datum.
- **Intent:** To inform/record (history and administration) — hostile or neutral, not to promote the movement, which is why they carry weight *against* a "pure invention" reading.

# 3. Limitations (what this record does NOT establish)
- They establish that, by the early second century, **Christians existed, worshipped Christ, and traced a founder executed under Pilate** — they do **not** independently corroborate the Gospel narrative, miracles, or resurrection (bracketed as metaphysical claims).
- The **Testimonium** is textually corrupted; only a reconstructed core is usable (SRC-0098), and a minority holds it wholly interpolated.
- Tacitus and Pliny are early-second-century; their value is as *early external confirmation of the movement's existence and belief*, not as first-generation evidence.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Tacitus *Ann.* 15.44: "Christus, from whom the name [Christian] had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty... at the hands of... Pontius Pilatus" — widely accepted as authentic and the strongest independent Roman notice.
- Josephus *Ant.* 20.200: James "the brother of Jesus who was called Christ" — most scholars accept as authentic; the more secure Josephan datum.
- Josephus *Ant.* 18.63–64 (TF): partially authentic (SRC-0098); the confessional clauses are interpolations.
- Pliny *Ep.* 10.96: Christians in Bithynia c. 112 CE "sang a hymn to Christ as to a god" — attests the movement's existence and worship, not the historical Jesus directly.

# 6. Verification (STD-0006 §5.7 / §7.5)
**Live-verified this session (strong).** The Tacitus *Ann.* 15.44 authenticity consensus and content, the TF partial-authenticity consensus, and the *Ant.* 20.200 James notice were confirmed against multiple independent scholarly summaries this session. **Pliny *Ep.* 10.96** — originally self-graded parametric by the Specialist — was **independently re-located during Critical Review – RQ-0013** (Wikipedia "Pliny the Younger on Christians"; Georgetown text): to Trajan, Bithynia, the verbatim phrase *"carmen Christo quasi deo"* ("a hymn to Christ as to a god"), c. 111–113 CE, authenticity not seriously disputed — **upgraded parametric → live-verified**. Reference locations (*Ann.* 15.44; *Ant.* 18.63–64, 20.200; *Ep.* 10.96) are the standard citations. No fabrication; no quotation is asserted beyond the standard renderings.
