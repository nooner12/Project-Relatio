# SRC-0071

# Collins — The Apocalyptic Imagination: An Introduction to Jewish Apocalyptic Literature

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
John J. Collins, *The Apocalyptic Imagination* — the **1984 Crossroad first edition** carried the subtitle *"An Introduction to the Jewish Matrix of Christianity"*; the later **Eerdmans editions** (2nd ed. 1998; 3rd ed. 2016) carry *"An Introduction to Jewish Apocalyptic Literature."* (Both subtitles are cited across the KB; CLM-0058 relies on the first-edition "Jewish matrix of Christianity" phrasing, which is this edition.) The standard survey of the Second Temple Jewish apocalyptic corpus (Enochic literature, Daniel, Qumran, and the early-Christian matrix). **In this KB it is the Second-Temple-side anchor and the measured voice on Persian influence:** Collins treats Persian (and Babylonian) materials as part of the milieu whose "codes and raw materials" the earliest apocalypses drew on, while insisting on the *distinctiveness* of the Jewish texts and grounding their emergence in the datable Hellenistic-era crises (esp. Daniel, 2nd c. BCE). He is a principal source for the internal-development alternative and for the Nazarene-terminus inheritance.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction), grounded in close reading of datable Jewish primary texts (H-a on the Jewish side).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very high — Collins (Yale) is the leading authority on the genre; his SBL genre definition of "apocalypse" is standard.
- **Transparency:** High — carefully distinguishes what the Jewish texts share with the milieu from what is distinctive, and dates the corpus firmly (Daniel 165 BCE; Book of the Watchers 3rd c. BCE).
- **Independence:** High — neither maximalist nor eliminativist on Persian influence; his default is the internal Jewish matrix with milieu inputs acknowledged.
- **Intent:** To characterize the genre and trace its development within Second Temple Judaism into early Christianity.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Collins does not adjudicate the *Iranian* dating problem from the Iranian side — he treats Persian material as milieu, not as a demonstrated source, and leaves the directional question largely open/cautious.
- His firm dating is for the *Jewish* corpus; it constrains, but does not by itself settle, the Persian-priority question.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The datable anchors of the Jewish apocalyptic corpus: Book of the Watchers (3rd c. BCE), Daniel 7–12 (~165 BCE), Qumran (2nd–1st c. BCE).
- Persian material belongs to the milieu but the Jewish texts are distinctive and emerge from datable Hellenistic-era crises (internal-development alternative).
- The apocalyptic frame of the earliest Jesus movement is inherited from this Jewish stream (the "Jewish matrix of Christianity").
