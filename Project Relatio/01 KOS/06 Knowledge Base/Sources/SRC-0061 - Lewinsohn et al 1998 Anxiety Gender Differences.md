# SRC-0061

# Lewinsohn et al. 1998 — Gender Differences in Anxiety Disorders and Anxiety Symptoms in Adolescents

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Lewinsohn, P. M., Gotlib, I. H., Lewinsohn, M., Seeley, J. R., & Allen, N. B. (1998), "Gender differences in anxiety disorders and anxiety symptoms in adolescents," *Journal of Abnormal Psychology* 107(1):109–117 (PubMed 9505043). Oregon Adolescent Depression Project sample; examined the female/male ratio in anxiety across development, including **retrospectively dated ages of onset**. Reports that, on retrospective data, **females were already about twice as likely as males to have had an anxiety disorder by age 6** — well before the pubertal window in which the depression ratio emerges. Citation and the age-6 finding verified this session via PubMed 9505043.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Construct-provenance code: `CONFLATED`.** The study uses the recorded **female/male category** and **retrospectively self-reported** onset ages; it assesses no biological mechanism and measures no gender-socialization variable. Sex labels are applied to what are effectively self-report/recall data, with no sex-vs-gender distinction drawn. It **can support only a gender-patterned (or unattributed) timing claim** (operative rule §3.1), never a sex-attributed one. Justification: demographic tag plus retrospective recall, no construct assessed.
- **Authority:** Moderate–High — well-regarded epidemiological cohort, peer-reviewed.
- **Transparency:** High — reports the age-graded ratio explicitly.
- **Independence:** Moderate — a single longitudinal project (Oregon), one sample.
- **Intent:** Characterize when and how much the anxiety female/male gap appears.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **Retrospective onset dating** is subject to recall error, telescoping, and gendered reporting differences — so the specific "age 6" figure is a soft anchor, not an incident-onset measurement.
- **Single sample, single region.** The precise age is not a cross-nationally established constant; what is more robust across the literature is the *direction* (anxiety's female skew appears early / pre-pubertally) rather than the exact age.
- **No mechanism, no gender measure.** Cannot say whether the early divergence reflects differential exposure, socialization, reporting, or biology.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- On retrospective data, the female preponderance in anxiety is **present by early childhood (~age 6)** — a *different, earlier* pattern than depression's pubertal emergence.
- The anxiety gap is broad across anxiety-disorder subtypes.
