# SRC-0108

# Behistun Inscription of Darius I

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
The Behistun (Bisotun) inscription, conventionally cited as **DB** — a monumental trilingual rock inscription and relief commissioned by Darius I, carved high on a cliff face at Bisotun in western Iran, narrating his accession and the suppression of the revolts that followed it. The text is in Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian.

Catalogued here as a **primary, material, contemporaneous** witness — royal epigraphy in the king's own official voice, physically extant.

**Corpus rule applied.** This record is the *object*. The critical edition through which the Old Persian text is read is catalogued separately as **SRC-0109 (Kent 1953)** — see §5. The two carry different evaluations: this record's trust question is authenticity and contemporaneity; the edition's is currency.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very high *for what it actually attests* — namely what Darius publicly proclaimed. It is contemporaneous, official, and physically extant, which places it in the strongest class of evidence available for the Achaemenid period.
- **Transparency:** High as an artifact — the monument is public, in situ, and thoroughly published. Note the deliberate inaccessibility of its placement: it was carved to be seen and not read closely, which is itself a fact about its rhetorical function.
- **Independence:** **None, and this is the decisive limitation.** It is royal self-presentation by the interested party. Its independence value is nil; its contemporaneity value is very high. These must not be conflated.
- **Intent:** Legitimation. The inscription exists to justify Darius's seizure of power and to establish his account of the revolts as the official one. Every substantive claim in it is made by a ruler with an overwhelming interest in its acceptance.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It attests **what Darius professed**, not the truth of what he narrates. The account of the revolts and of Gaumata is precisely the kind of contested royal narrative that contemporaneity does not validate.
- Darius's invocation of Ahura Mazda establishes his **public profession**, not that Achaemenid royal religion was "Zoroastrianism" in any doctrinal sense. The relationship between Achaemenid Mazda-worship and the Avestan tradition is a separate and contested question this inscription cannot settle.
- It bears on **neither** Zoroaster's date **nor** the Avesta's composition or transmission.
- Its religious content is incidental to its purpose; it is a political document that mentions a god, not a religious text.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Darius's repeated invocation of Ahura Mazda as the source of his kingship and success.
- The trilingual character of the monument (Old Persian, Elamite, Babylonian).

# 6. Verification (STD-0006 §5.7 / §7.5)
**Not verified — parametric.** The inscription was **not re-located** during the authoring session; it is described from the author-model's parametric knowledge. The monument's existence, location, trilingual character, and attribution to Darius I are not seriously in doubt in the field, but that is background consensus rather than verification performed here. The **carving date is given as approximate (c. 520 BCE) and its precise establishment is unknown**; individual composers are unknown. Verification strength: **weak** as a performed check. Live re-verification is required before load-bearing use.
