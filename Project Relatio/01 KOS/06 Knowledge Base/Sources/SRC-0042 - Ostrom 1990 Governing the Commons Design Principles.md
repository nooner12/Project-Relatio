# SRC-0042

# Ostrom 1990 — Governing the Commons (Design Principles for Enduring Institutions)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Ostrom, E. (1990), *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action*, Cambridge University Press. The Nobel-recognised comparative study from which Ostrom induced **design principles** distinguishing long-enduring self-governed common-pool-resource institutions from ones that failed. It anchors the **synthesis / governance** claim: formal rules endure and add value when they are locally fitted (congruent), monitored and enforced proportionately, and — critically — **revisable by those they govern** through collective-choice arrangements nested across levels.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very High — Ostrom received the 2009 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences substantially for this work; the design principles have been widely re-tested (e.g., Cox, Arnold & Villamayor-Tomás 2010 broadly upheld them).
- **Transparency:** High — the principles are derived by explicit comparative case analysis and stated as a numbered list.
- **Independence:** High — academic; the conclusion cuts against both pure-market and pure-state prescriptions.
- **Intent:** Inform / theorise institutional design.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- The principles were **induced from common-pool-resource cases** (fisheries, forests, irrigation, groundwater). Transfer to *knowledge* systems and standards governance is an **analogical extension**, not something Ostrom demonstrates for that domain.
- They are **associated with** enduring institutions (largely by induction/comparison), not proven as sufficient causes; later work treats them as robust correlates with boundary conditions, not laws.
- Ostrom herself warned against treating the principles as a **blueprint / panacea** — they are diagnostic regularities, not a formula.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Design principles of long-enduring self-governed commons include, among others: **clearly defined boundaries**; **congruence** between rules and local conditions (and between costs and benefits); **collective-choice arrangements** (those affected by rules can participate in modifying them); **monitoring**; **graduated sanctions**; **conflict-resolution mechanisms**; **recognition of rights to organize**; and, for larger systems, **nested enterprises** (governance organized in multiple layers). *(Principle set verified this session via multiple scholarly summaries; exact wording paraphrased.)*
- Central lesson used here: rules that are **participatory to revise, locally congruent, and nested** are the ones that persist and adapt — the governance conditions under which a formal constraint keeps earning its place.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0033 (constraints capture value without ossifying when enabling, locally fitted, and revisable through participatory, multi-level governance).
