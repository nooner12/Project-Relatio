# SRC-0092

# Nongbri 2005 — The Use and Abuse of P52

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Brent Nongbri, "The Use and Abuse of P52: Papyrological Pitfalls in the Dating of the Fourth Gospel," *Harvard Theological Review* 98:1 (2005): 23–48. The paper that recalibrated the field's use of **P52 (Rylands Greek Papyrus 457)**, the small fragment of John 18 long cited (following C. H. Roberts, 1935) as the earliest NT manuscript, c. 125 CE. In this KB it is the **methodological caution against precise palaeographic dating** — the check on apologetic over-early dating of the fragment and, through it, of John.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction) — a palaeographic critical argument about the H-b (material/papyrological) evidence.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — Nongbri is a specialist in early Christian papyrology (Yale PhD; author of *God's Library*); the article is now the standard citation for the caution.
- **Transparency:** High — publishes images of the dated comparator hands and argues explicitly from the width of the palaeographic window.
- **Independence:** High — cuts against the confessional interest in an early John; no apologetic program.
- **Intent:** To discipline inference — to show that palaeography cannot fix a fragment to a 25-year window as confidently as the literature assumed.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It does **not** re-date P52 *late*; it shows the *range* of plausible dates is wider than claimed — comparanda support a hand anywhere across roughly the whole second century (and possibly into the early third).
- It bears on **one fragment**; it does not overturn the broader manuscript picture or the mainstream dating of John (c. 90–110 CE) established on other grounds.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Roberts's early-second-century assignment can be confirmed as *possible* but not as *precise*: dated comparator hands similar to P52 run as late as c. 152 CE (P. Mich. inv. 5336) and c. 184 CE (P. Amh. 2.78).
- Palaeographic dating of a small literary fragment carries a margin of several decades at least; a single "c. 125 CE" point-date is methodologically unsound.
