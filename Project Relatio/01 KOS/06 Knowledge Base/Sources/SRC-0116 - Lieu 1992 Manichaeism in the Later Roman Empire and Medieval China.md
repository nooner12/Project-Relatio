# Lieu 1992 — Manichaeism in the Later Roman Empire and Medieval China

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Samuel N. C. Lieu, *Manichaeism in the Later Roman Empire and Medieval China*, 2nd edition (1992).

The standard synthetic history of Manichaeism's diffusion westward into the Roman Empire and eastward into China. In the Iranian-family source base it is cited in connection with both the Diocletianic rescript (SRC-0115) and the survival of Manichaean worship at Cao'an in Fujian (SRC-0117).

**This is a secondary study, not an edition.** It is explicitly *not* an edition of the Cao'an inscription and not an edition of the *Collatio*; no object/edition relationship is asserted to either.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High. Lieu is the leading historian of Manichaean diffusion and this monograph is the standard synthesis on its subject.
- **Transparency:** High. Extensively documented, with the primary material cited throughout, so its judgments can be traced back to the evidence.
- **Independence:** Independent scholarly work, external to the tradition.
- **Intent:** Historical synthesis — to give a connected account across a very wide geographical and chronological span.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **It is a synthesis, and syntheses adjudicate.** On contested points Lieu reports and weighs positions; for load-bearing use the primary editions and specialist literature should be consulted directly rather than the synthesis relied on as a witness.
- It is **not a primary witness to anything**. Where it discusses the Cao'an material or the rescript, its authority is derivative of the primary evidence catalogued at SRC-0117 and SRC-0115.
- The breadth that makes it valuable also means **uneven depth**: no single work can be equally authoritative across late Roman North Africa and Yuan-era Fujian.
- Published 1992; subsequent work — particularly on the Chinese material, which has developed considerably — is not reflected.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The Roman-empire suppression context surrounding the Diocletianic rescript.
- The account of Manichaean persistence in medieval and late-imperial China, including the Fujian material.
