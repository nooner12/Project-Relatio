# SRC-0093

# Ehrman — The New Testament: A Historical Introduction (OUP)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Bart D. Ehrman, *The New Testament: A Historical Introduction to the Early Christian Writings* (Oxford University Press; 1st ed. 1997, through multiple later editions). The most widely-used secular university textbook on the NT in the English-speaking world. In this KB it anchors the **mainstream critical-consensus positions** on Gospel dating, the anonymity of the Gospels and their later attribution, and the undisputed/disputed/pseudonymous partition of the Pauline corpus. Ehrman writes from an explicitly non-confessional, historical-critical standpoint.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction) — a critical textbook synthesizing dating/authorship arguments.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very high — Ehrman (UNC Chapel Hill) is a leading NT scholar; the textbook is field-standard and peer-adopted.
- **Transparency:** High — states evidence and reasoning for each dating/authorship judgment; distinguishes consensus from his own view.
- **Independence:** High — non-confessional; his conclusions frequently cut against traditional Christian attributions, and are cited by critics *and* opponents.
- **Intent:** To inform/teach; a textbook, not a polemic (contrast his trade books, which are more argumentative).

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- A textbook reports and weighs scholarly reconstruction; the dating/authorship judgments it carries are **H-e reconstructions**, not documented facts, however strong the consensus.
- Ehrman's framing occasionally leans skeptical; where he reports a consensus it is checkable against Brown (SRC-0094) and Sanders (SRC-0097), used here as independent cross-checks.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Gospel dating consensus: Mark c. 65–70; Matthew and Luke c. 80–85/90; John c. 90–95/110.
- The Gospels are formally **anonymous**; the names (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) are second-century attributions, not internal claims.
- The Pauline corpus divides: seven undisputed letters; three disputed (Ephesians, Colossians, 2 Thessalonians); the three Pastorals (1–2 Timothy, Titus) widely judged pseudonymous.
