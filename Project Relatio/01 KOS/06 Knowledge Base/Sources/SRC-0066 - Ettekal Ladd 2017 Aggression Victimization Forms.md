# SRC-0066

# Ettekal & Ladd 2017 — Developmental Continuity and Change in Physical, Verbal, and Relational Aggression and Peer Victimization

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Ettekal, I., & Ladd, G. W. (2017), "Developmental continuity and change in physical, verbal and relational aggression and peer victimization from childhood to adolescence," *Developmental Psychology* 53(9):1709–1721, DOI 10.1037/dev0000357. Longitudinal study spanning grades 1–11. Relevant to INV-0010's **secondary (modal-expression) question**: aggression/victimization diverges by **form** along sex-categorized lines — boys overrepresented in physical (and high-across-forms) profiles; a **form-specific, mostly-girls profile of primarily relational** aggression/victimization emerges by late childhood and grows into adolescence. Citation and the form-specific findings verified this session via the PMC full text (PMC5600622).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Construct-provenance code: `CONFLATED`.** Sex is used as a **covariate/grouping category**; the study does not measure gender identity, role, or socialization, and assesses no biological mechanism. The form divergence is described by sex label. It **can support only a gender-patterned (or unattributed) form-divergence claim** (operative rule §3.1). Justification: sex as covariate on behavioral profiles, no construct measured. (This divergence is *strongly* theorized as gender-socialization-driven, but this source does not measure that, so it does not upgrade to GENDER-MEASURED.)
- **Authority:** High — top developmental journal, established authors on peer relations.
- **Transparency:** High — reports form-specific developmental profiles.
- **Independence:** High relative to the other domains.
- **Intent:** Map developmental continuity/change in aggression and victimization by form.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **Mean-level form differences can be small.** The broader literature notes the physical-vs-relational sex difference, while directionally consistent, is modest in magnitude; this is a form/modal-expression divergence, not a large-effect claim.
- **Not an onset-timing study for a disorder.** It addresses *form* of a stressor (peer victimization), directly serving the secondary question but not the primary onset-timing question.
- **No gender measure, no mechanism** — cannot distinguish socialization from any other driver.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Boys overrepresented in physical and high-across-forms aggression/victimization profiles.
- A **mostly-girls, primarily relational** profile emerges by late childhood and increases into adolescence.
- Form divergence is developmentally patterned — it consolidates rather than being fixed from the start.
