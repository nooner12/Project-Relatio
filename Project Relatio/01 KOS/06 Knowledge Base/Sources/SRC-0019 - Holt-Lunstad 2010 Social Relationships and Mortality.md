# SRC-0019

# Holt-Lunstad, Smith & Layton (2010) — Social Relationships and Mortality Risk (Meta-Analytic Review)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Landmark meta-analytic review, *PLOS Medicine* (2010), quantifying the association between social relationships and mortality, and framing it partly through the **stress-buffering** model (social resources dampen neuroendocrine responses to chronic stressors).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very High — highly cited meta-analysis; the reference point for the social-connection/health literature.
- **Transparency:** High — large pooled sample, reported moderators.
- **Independence:** High.
- **Intent:** Inform.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **Observational.** It establishes a strong *association* between social connection and survival, **not** that a deliberate intervention to increase connection produces durable stress relief. Reverse causation and confounding (healthier/less-stressed people sustain more relationships) are not excluded.
- Mortality, not "chronic stress," is the primary endpoint; the stress-buffering link is theoretical/mechanistic here.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- **148 studies, 308,849 participants; ~50% increased odds of survival for those with stronger social relationships (OR ≈ 1.50).**
- The authors liken the magnitude to established mortality risk factors (e.g. smoking).
- Stress-buffering model: social support provides informational/emotional/tangible resources that promote adaptive neuroendocrine responses to stressors.
