# SRC-0017

# Cochrane — Organisational-Level Interventions for Reducing Occupational Stress

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Cochrane systematic review (**Ruotsalainen et al., 2015, Cochrane Database Syst Rev — exact DOI to confirm**) of interventions — including **organisational-level** changes (work schedules, workload, support structures, work methods) — to reduce occupational stress in healthcare workers, the "reduce the stressor" counterpart to the individual-level review (SRC-0016). NIOSH published commentary comparing the two ("changing the organization or educating staff?").

> **Citation correction (Critical Review – RQ-0005 §3, §9):** the previously-cited **PMC6494543 is the PROTOCOL only** (Giga et al. 2018, Cochrane CD013014) and reports no results. The conclusions summarised in this record belong to **Ruotsalainen et al. (2015)**. Exact DOI/edition of the Ruotsalainen 2015 review still to be confirmed (no web access this session; KOS-0003 §12.1 — uncertainty stated, not fabricated).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **The organizational-intervention evidence base is thin and mixed** — this review's honest conclusion is close to a null for most organisational changes, which limits how strongly stressor-reduction can be *empirically* endorsed (see CLM-0017).
- **Exact DOI/edition of the Ruotsalainen 2015 review remains to be confirmed** (no web access this session) — flagged per KOS-0003 §12.1; author attribution itself is corrected and verified via the Critical Review (§3).
- Healthcare-worker population.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- **Changing work schedules** may reduce stress (low-quality evidence); **most other organisational interventions showed no clear effect** on stress levels.
- Read together with SRC-0016, the pattern is the paradox at the heart of CLM-0017: stressor reduction is *conceptually* central to durable relief, yet the organisational-intervention *trials* are fewer, harder, and weaker than the individual-technique trials.
