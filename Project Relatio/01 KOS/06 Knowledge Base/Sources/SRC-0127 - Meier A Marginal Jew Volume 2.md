# SRC-0127

# Meier — A Marginal Jew, vol. 2: Mentor, Message, and Miracles (1994)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
John P. Meier, *A Marginal Jew: Rethinking the Historical Jesus, vol. 2: Mentor, Message, and Miracles* (Anchor Bible Reference Library; New York: Doubleday, 1994; ISBN 978-0-385-46992-0; the series later reissued by Yale University Press). A **distinct work** from SRC-0098 (vol. 1, 1991 — sources and method), catalogued separately per the corpus rule that different volumes carrying different arguments are separate evaluable records. Vol. 2 is the field's most exhaustive criteria-driven treatment of exactly INV-0014's territory: Part 1 **John the Baptist and Jesus' baptism** ("mentor"), Part 2 **the Kingdom-of-God proclamation** ("message"), Part 3 **the miracle tradition** — healings and exorcisms as *reputed deeds*, with the events' supernatural character explicitly beyond historical adjudication ("miracles").

**H-band of this source (prose only):** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction, criteria-driven).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — the standard exhaustive treatment; cited across the field including by critics of its method.
- **Transparency:** High — every judgment is argued through named criteria with the counter-cases stated.
- **Independence:** Moderate-to-High — Meier is a Catholic priest, but the work's method brackets faith claims by design (the famous "unpapal conclave" standard: conclusions an honest Catholic, Protestant, Jew, and agnostic could share); its miracle treatment grades *repute*, not supernatural events.
- **Intent:** Criteria-rigorous reconstruction; deliberately minimalist about what passes.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Its method is the criteria program at full strength — exactly what SRC-0124 critiques; criteria-derived conclusions are graded here with that discount in view.
- Establishes scholarly judgments (H-e), not new primary attestation; the attestation layer is INV-0013's.
- The miracle discussion establishes *that the tradition reports deeds and that the repute is early and multiply attested* — explicitly **not** that any miracle occurred (Meier's own boundary, matching this investigation's bracket).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The baptism of Jesus by John as among the most certain facts about Jesus, argued centrally from the **criterion of embarrassment**: Jesus voluntarily submitting to John's baptism of repentance for the remission of sins was an embarrassment "each evangelist tries to neutralize in his own way" (Mark states it plainly; Matthew adds John's protest; Luke displaces it; John's Gospel omits the act itself).
- John as the greatest single influence on Jesus ("in a sense, Jesus never was without John").
- The Kingdom of God as the central, pervasive theme of Jesus' proclamation, attested across all strands of the tradition.
- The miracle tradition as too broadly attested (Mark, Q, M, L, John; and the hostile Beelzebul charge) to be a late accretion in toto: the *repute* as exorcist/healer belongs to the earliest recoverable layer, while the events' character is beyond the historian's tools.
