# SRC-0115

# Collatio Legum Mosaicarum et Romanarum 15.3

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
*Collatio legum Mosaicarum et Romanarum* ("Comparison of Mosaic and Roman Laws"), section **15.3** — an anonymous late antique compilation juxtaposing Mosaic and Roman legal provisions, which preserves the text of the imperial rescript ordering the suppression of the Manichaeans.

Catalogued as a **primary text**: it is the principal witness to the rescript's wording, which is otherwise lost.

**No object/edition split made.** The source base names the work and section only; **no distinct critical edition or translation is cited**. Per the corpus rule's constraint, no edition has been invented in order to force a split — this is a single record.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High for the rescript's text, by necessity — it is effectively the only substantial witness. Authority here derives from uniqueness as much as from quality, which is a weaker footing than it first appears.
- **Transparency:** **Low, and this is the notable weakness.** The compilation does not disclose its own date, authorship, or principles of selection; the compiler's purposes are inferred by scholars, not stated. A source that transmits a document without explaining its own provenance offers limited means of checking its transmission.
- **Independence:** **Strong, and this is its real evidential value.** The compilation is external to Manichaeism — a Roman legal work with a Jewish-Christian comparative frame. Attestation of the movement's suppression from a source with no stake in Manichaean self-presentation is worth considerably more than in-group testimony.
- **Intent:** Legal-comparative, with an apologetic cast. Its purpose is to juxtapose legal traditions, not to document Manichaeism; the rescript appears incidentally, which somewhat reduces the risk of tendentious shaping of *that* text.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It transmits the rescript **at a remove**: this is a later compilation, not a chancery original or an archival copy. Textual transmission through an undated compilation of unknown editorial principles introduces real uncertainty.
- The rescript's **date is contested** (297 vs. 302 CE); the compilation does not settle it.
- The rescript's **authenticity is broadly but not universally accepted**. Broad acceptance is the current scholarly position, not a demonstrated result, and the record should not present it as beyond dispute.
- It attests an **imperial order**, not its enforcement, reach, or effect. What a rescript commands and what happened are different questions.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- *Collatio* 15.3: the text of the Diocletianic rescript against the Manichaeans.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- Catalogued alongside SRC-0116 (Lieu 1992), the standard secondary study through which this material is conventionally discussed; `related_documents` only. **Lieu is explicitly not an edition of this text** and no `derived_from` edge is asserted.

# 6. Verification (STD-0006 §5.7 / §7.5)
**Not verified — parametric.** Neither the compilation nor the rescript text was **re-located** during the authoring session; this record is written from the author-model's parametric knowledge. The work's character, the section reference, and the contested dating are recorded as standardly reported in the field. The **compilation's own date and its compiler are unknown**, and no edition, publisher, or modern translation has been supplied, none being named in the source base. Verification strength: **weak**.
