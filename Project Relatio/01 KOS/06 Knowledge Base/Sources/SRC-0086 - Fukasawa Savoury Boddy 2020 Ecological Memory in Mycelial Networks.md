# SRC-0086

# Fukasawa, Savoury & Boddy 2020 — "Ecological Memory and Relocation Decisions in Fungal Mycelial Networks"

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Yu Fukasawa, Melanie Savoury & Lynne Boddy, "Ecological memory and relocation decisions in fungal mycelial networks: responses to quantity and location of new resources," *The ISME Journal* 14(2):380–388 (2020; online 2019). When inocula were severed from a newly colonized wood resource and re-set in fresh soil, hyphae **preferentially regrew from the side that had faced the previous resource** — a directional "memory"/carry-over effect. **In this KB it is the anchor for the fungal-specific memory-like datum in CLM-0064** — and, crucially, it is *fungal* (not *Physarum*) evidence.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High. Peer-reviewed in a leading microbial-ecology journal; Boddy's group.
- **Transparency:** High. Clear experimental manipulation; the authors explicitly discuss **what kind of "memory" this is** (external cue vs. somatic/physiological vs. structural carry-over).
- **Independence:** The authors frame it as "a first step in the study of non-neuronal intelligence" — a mildly enthusiast framing to weigh; but the *datum* (directional regrowth) is straightforwardly measured.
- **Intent:** To test whether prior growth history biases subsequent foraging decisions.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Establishes a **directional carry-over / "ecological memory" effect**; the authors themselves flag **semantic ambiguity** in calling it "memory" and offer three non-committal mechanisms — so it does **NOT** establish learning, recall, or cognitive memory in the neural sense.
- Does not demonstrate habituation, associative learning, or anticipation; the memory shown is a **structural/physiological bias**, not stored representational content.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Hyphae **regrew preferentially from the side that originally faced the new bait**, retaining spatial directional information.
- The authors propose **external / somatic / structural-carry-over** as candidate mechanisms and explicitly treat "memory" as an operational term — "recognizing such a carry-over effect as a kind of memory is a first step in the study of non-neuronal intelligence."
