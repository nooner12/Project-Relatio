# SRC-0048

# DuBois et al. 2011 — How Effective Are Mentoring Programs for Youth?

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
DuBois, D. L., Portillo, N., Rhodes, J. E., Silverthorn, N., & Valentine, J. C. (2011), "How Effective Are Mentoring Programs for Youth? A Systematic Assessment of the Evidence," *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* 12(2):57–91. A **meta-analysis of 73 independent evaluations** (comparison-group studies) of youth mentoring programs. Primary anchor for domain 4 (mentoring). Verified this session via SAGE record and multiple secondary summaries; a companion earlier meta-analysis by the same lead author (DuBois et al., 2002) reported a comparable small effect.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed meta-analysis in a policy-facing journal; authors are the field's leading mentoring researchers.
- **Transparency:** High — inclusion criteria (non-parental adult, non-professional helping role, ≤18, comparison group) and moderator analyses reported.
- **Independence:** Moderate — the authors are advocates *and* critics of mentoring; the paper is notably candid about small average effects, which supports its credibility.
- **Intent:** Assess effectiveness and identify what makes programs work; inform practice.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- The **overall average effect is small (d ≈ 0.21)**; mentoring is not a strong universal intervention, and effects are **heterogeneous**.
- Establishes **moderators** (matching on interests; program adherence to best practices; relationship strength; higher-risk youth benefit more) but these are **correlational within the meta-analysis**, not experimentally isolated.
- Does not establish durability, nor that any *specific* program design (e.g., skills-based heritage mentoring) achieves above-average effects.
- Primary text is paywalled; **d ≈ 0.21** and the moderator pattern were corroborated across multiple secondary summaries, not read from the primary PDF this session.
- **Funding:** not verified this session (DuBois's mentoring work has drawn US federal/foundation youth-development funding historically; state as unverified).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Overall effect size **d ≈ 0.21** — "small but significant" — across 73 evaluations.
- Effects **enhanced** when programs use more theory-/evidence-based best practices and when strong mentor–youth relationships form; **matching on interests** roughly doubled the effect in the reported moderator analysis (≈.41 vs ≈.20).
- **Higher-risk / disadvantaged** youth appeared most likely to benefit.
