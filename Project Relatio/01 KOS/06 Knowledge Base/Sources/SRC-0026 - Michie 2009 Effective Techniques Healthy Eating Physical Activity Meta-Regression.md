# SRC-0026

# Michie et al. 2009 — Effective Techniques in Healthy Eating and Physical Activity Interventions (Meta-Regression)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Meta-regression linking specific behaviour-change techniques to intervention effectiveness in healthy-eating and physical-activity interventions: Michie, Abraham, Whittington, McAteer & Gupta (2009), *Health Psychology* 28(6):690–701. 122 evaluations, N ≈ 44,747. It is the primary evidence that **self-monitoring** is a high-value ingredient.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed meta-regression by the group that later built the Behaviour Change Technique Taxonomy.
- **Transparency:** High — technique coding scheme and meta-regression reported; heterogeneity (I²≈69%) disclosed.
- **Independence:** High — academic.
- **Intent:** Inform.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Overall pooled effect is **small (≈0.31 SMD)** — techniques help, but modestly.
- Meta-regression is **observational across trials**: technique–outcome associations are correlational and subject to confounding by intervention quality/intensity, not a randomised head-to-head of techniques.
- Outcomes are eating/activity behaviour, largely **short-to-medium term**; it does not establish long-term (>12 month) **maintenance**.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Overall pooled effect size **0.31 (95% CI 0.26–0.36, I²≈69%)** across 122 evaluations (N≈44,747). *(Verified this session via PubMed 19916637 / DARE abstract.)*
- **Self-monitoring** explained the greatest share of between-study heterogeneity (~13%).
- Interventions combining **self-monitoring with at least one other control-theory (self-regulation) technique** were significantly more effective than others: **0.42 vs 0.26**. *(Verified this session.)*

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0023 (self-monitoring + self-regulation as best-evidenced technique cluster).
