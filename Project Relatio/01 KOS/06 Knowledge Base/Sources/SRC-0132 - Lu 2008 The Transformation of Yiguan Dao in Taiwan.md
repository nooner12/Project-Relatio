# SRC-0132

# Lu (2008) — The Transformation of Yiguan Dao in Taiwan: Adapting to a Changing Religious Economy

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Yunfeng Lu, *The Transformation of Yiguan Dao in Taiwan: Adapting to a Changing Religious Economy* (Lanham: Lexington Books, 2008; ISBN 978-0-7391-1719-4). The **first English-language scholarly monograph devoted exclusively to Yiguandao** (based on 2002 fieldwork), analyzing the movement through the religious-economy model: its operation under KMT suppression, its rivalry dynamics in Taiwan's religious market, its growth into **the largest sect in Taiwan**, and its **1987 legalization**. Lu is a sociologist of religion at Peking University. In this KB it anchors the trajectory facts (suppression → growth → 1987 legal status) and the sociological register in which the movement is analyzed — as a **sect in a religious market**, not as a school of an institutional tradition.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — first dedicated anglophone monograph; peer-reviewed press; author a specialist sociologist of Chinese religion (Peking University; Baylor ISR affiliation).
- **Transparency:** High — fieldwork-based with a declared theoretical model.
- **Independence:** High — sociological analysis; no confessional stake.
- **Intent:** Scholarly — to extend the religious-economy model to Chinese societies via the Yiguandao case.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **Book interior not independently read this session** (see §6): trajectory facts are consumed from open review summaries and the publisher record; chapter-level content not asserted.
- Its analytical vocabulary ("sect," religious-economy terms) is theory-laden; consumed as evidence of the field's register, not as a criteria argument.
- 2002 fieldwork: pre-dates the movement's most recent developments (SRC-0131 covers those).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Trajectory: Yiguandao developed into the largest sect in Taiwan despite KMT suppression; legal status gained 1987 (CLM-0081).
- Register: the movement analyzed as a sect/new religious competitor in a religious economy — a further instance of the field *not* treating it as a Daoist school (CLM-0083 census; CLM-0082 organizational features).
