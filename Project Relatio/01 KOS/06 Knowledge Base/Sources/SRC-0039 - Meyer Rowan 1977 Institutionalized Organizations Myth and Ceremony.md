# SRC-0039

# Meyer & Rowan 1977 — Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Meyer, J. W., & Rowan, B. (1977), "Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony," *American Journal of Sociology* 83(2):340–363. A foundational neo-institutionalist theory article. It anchors the **decoupling** failure mode: organizations adopt formal structures (rules, procedures, credentials) to secure *legitimacy* rather than *efficiency*, and then buffer their actual work from those structures — so a formal constraint can be present and honoured ceremonially while not governing real practice.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very High — one of the most-cited papers in organizational sociology; a seminal statement of institutional theory.
- **Transparency:** High for a theory paper — the mechanism (rationalized myths → adoption → decoupling → confidence/good faith) is laid out clearly.
- **Independence:** High — academic.
- **Intent:** Inform / theorise.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It is a **theoretical account**, not an empirical measurement of *how often* decoupling occurs or how large its effects are. Decoupling has since been documented empirically by others, but this paper does not quantify it.
- Its claim is that structures **can** be decoupled and often are for institutional reasons — **not** that all formal structure is ceremonial. Over-reading it into "formalization is mere theatre" misrepresents it.
- Written about mid-20th-century formal organizations; application to modern knowledge architectures and metadata regimes is an analogical extension.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Many formal structures are **rationalized institutional myths**: adopted because the wider environment treats them as legitimate/proper, independent of whether they improve outcomes.
- Organizations gain **legitimacy, resources, stability, and survival prospects** by conforming to such myths.
- To protect efficiency when myths conflict with practical demands, organizations **decouple** formal structure from actual activities and maintain them through a "logic of confidence and good faith" (ceremonial inspection/evaluation is minimized).
