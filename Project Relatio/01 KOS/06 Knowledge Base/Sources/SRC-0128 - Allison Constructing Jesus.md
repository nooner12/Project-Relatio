# SRC-0128

# Allison — Constructing Jesus: Memory, Imagination, and History (2010)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Dale C. Allison, Jr., *Constructing Jesus: Memory, Imagination, and History* (Grand Rapids: Baker Academic, 2010). The major **memory-skeptical** statement on the Jesus tradition: drawing on the psychology of memory, Allison argues that human recall — individual and collective — is too frail and too reconstructive for the criteria of authenticity to certify individual sayings; what memory *does* preserve reliably is **recurrent pattern and general impression**. His method therefore grades the tradition's **repeated, cross-strand impressions** (the "recurrent attestation" of themes such as eschatological urgency, the kingdom, exorcism-repute) while treating verdicts on single sayings as beyond the evidence. In this KB it is the harder-skeptical pole of the CLM-0079 transmission question, paired against Dunn (SRC-0126): more skeptical than Dunn about the tradition's fidelity, arguing that even a fixed apostolic-witness center does not escape the frailties of the memories that originated the tradition.

**H-band of this source (prose only):** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction informed by memory science).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — Allison is among the most respected historical-Jesus scholars; this is his major methodological statement.
- **Transparency:** High — the memory-science basis and its consequences are argued explicitly, with the author's own prior positions revised on the record.
- **Independence:** High — a Christian scholar whose argument *lowers* attainable confidence in saying-level authenticity; the deflationary direction runs against confessional interest, which strengthens it as a check.
- **Intent:** Methodological reorientation — from authenticating items to weighing recurrent impressions.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It does not establish that the tradition is *unreliable wholesale* — Allison's point is that the reliable layer is the pattern, not the item; wholesale skepticism is as unwarranted as item-level confidence.
- The memory-science transfer (from experimental psychology to first-century oral communities) is itself an inference, argued but not directly testable.
- It bears on the *teaching-material* claim's grade; it does not overturn the multiply-attested framework facts (existence, crucifixion, baptism, kingdom-proclamation as theme — which its own recurrent-attestation method supports).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Memory frailty: individual sayings cannot be certified by criteria; the criteria over-promise (converging with SRC-0124 from a different direction).
- Recurrent attestation: what appears repeatedly across independent strands and forms — the eschatological kingdom-proclamation, the parabolic/aphoristic teaching style, the exorcist-repute — reflects genuine memory of the characteristic Jesus, even where no single item is certifiable.
- Against Dunn: stable community tradition does not immunize the content against the reconstructive character of the originating memories.
