# SRC-0118

# Kreyenbroek 1995 — Yezidism: Its Background, Observances and Textual Tradition

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Philip G. Kreyenbroek, *Yezidism — Its Background, Observances and Textual Tradition* (1995); pages 27–44 cited in the source base.

A standard scholarly monograph on Yazidism covering its historical background, ritual observance, and oral textual tradition. In the source base it carries three distinct loads: the historical figure of Sheikh ʿAdī ibn Musāfir, the contested Iranian-substrate account of Yazidism's origins, and the text-critical assessment of the published Yazidi "sacred books."

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High. Kreyenbroek is a leading scholar of Yazidism and Iranian religions, and this is a standard monograph on a subject with relatively few authoritative treatments.
- **Transparency:** High. The oral textual material is presented and analysed rather than merely summarised, and the state of scholarly dispute is surveyed openly.
- **Independence:** Independent scholarly work — but conducted **in collaboration with community informants**, as work on a living oral tradition must be. That is methodologically appropriate and simultaneously a mediation to keep in view.
- **Intent:** Documentary and analytic — to record and interpret a tradition whose primary transmission is oral. **On the origins question specifically the intent is partly advocative**: Kreyenbroek argues the Iranian-substrate case rather than merely reporting it.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **The substrate thesis is advocacy, not neutral survey.** Kreyenbroek argues that Yazidism descends primarily from a pre-Islamic Western Iranian religion, against a competing account deriving it primarily from the Sufi tradition around Sheikh ʿAdī. He does survey the dispute, but this record must not treat his position as the settled scholarly view — there is no direct textual chain, and the reconstruction is comparative.
- **Oral tradition is mediated evidence.** Yazidi religious material is transmitted orally; any textual documentation is a scholarly artifact of the encounter, not a manuscript tradition, and questions of selection, informant variation, and recording conditions apply.
- Its strength is **uneven across its three uses**: the text-critical assessment of the published "sacred books" rests on documentary manuscript evidence and is much better grounded than the substrate reconstruction, which is comparative and contested. These should not be cited with the same weight.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Pages 27–44: the historical background, including Sheikh ʿAdī ibn Musāfir and the Lalish cult centre.
- The Iranian-substrate argument for Yazidism's origins, and the survey of the competing Sufi-origin account.
- The text-critical treatment of the published Yazidi "sacred books" and their provenance.
