# SRC-0032

# Eysenbach 2005 — The Law of Attrition

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Landmark viewpoint article naming and framing the "**law of attrition**" — the observation that in eHealth/digital-behaviour applications a substantial proportion of users disengage or drop out before completion: Eysenbach (2005), *Journal of Medical Internet Research* 7(1):e11. It distinguishes **non-usage attrition** (stopping use of the tool) from **dropout attrition** (leaving the study).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High as the originating conceptual statement (very heavily cited), from the founding editor of JMIR — but it is a **viewpoint/editorial**, not itself a meta-analysis of attrition rates.
- **Transparency:** High — argument and definitions explicit.
- **Independence:** High — academic.
- **Intent:** Inform / agenda-setting ("a science of attrition").

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It is **conceptual**, not a pooled quantitative estimate of a specific attrition rate; it establishes that early attrition is **characteristic and pervasive**, not a single number.
- It predates most modern app/wearable ecosystems; specific magnitudes must come from later empirical work (a flagged gap — no precise universal attrition statistic is asserted here).
- Attrition is a real-world adherence phenomenon, but its *causes* (usability, motivation, lack of results) are not fully resolved by this source.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- In digital/self-directed health interventions, **a substantial proportion of users drop out or stop using the application before completion** — a distinct, near-universal feature of eHealth trials, warranting a dedicated "science of attrition." *(Verified this session via PMC-1550631 / JMIR record.)*
- High dropout is often **under-reported** because researchers treat it as failure — meaning published efficacy can overstate real-world adherence.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0022 (early attrition is the baseline condition of self-directed/digital wellness interventions).
