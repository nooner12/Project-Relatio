# Ehrman — Did Jesus Exist? (2012)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Bart D. Ehrman, *Did Jesus Exist? The Historical Argument for Jesus of Nazareth* (HarperOne, 2012). The most prominent mainstream response to the mythicist ("no historical Jesus") position, written by a non-Christian, agnostic critical scholar. In this KB it represents the **field-consensus pole** on the historicity question — that a historical Jesus existed — and, importantly, its *argument*: that the consensus rests substantially on the early sources evaluated in this investigation (the undisputed Pauline letters, the multiple independent Gospel traditions, and the pre-literary material), not on faith. It is paired against SRC-0101 (Carrier) to steelman both poles.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction) — a critical-historical argument.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — Ehrman is a leading, non-confessional NT scholar; this is his direct treatment of the historicity question.
- **Transparency:** High — lays out the sources and the argument; explicitly non-confessional.
- **Independence:** High — an agnostic defending historicity *against* the mythicists checks the charge that historicity is a confessional posture; the near-universal scholarly consensus he reports spans atheist, Jewish, and Christian scholars.
- **Intent:** To persuade (against mythicism) — a trade book, argumentative in register, but reporting a genuine and overwhelming scholarly consensus.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It establishes the **existence** of a historical Jesus at the level of scholarly consensus; it does **not** establish the truth of any theological claim about him (bracketed).
- A trade book, more assertive than a monograph; its consensus report is checkable and is corroborated by independent surveys used this session.
- Consensus strength is recorded **separately** from evidence class (KOS-0003 §9): the consensus is near-universal even though the underlying evidence is H-e reconstruction from H-a/H-c sources.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Mythicism is a fringe position rejected by virtually all scholars of antiquity, across confessional and non-confessional backgrounds.
- The positive case rests on the early, multiply-attested sources: Paul (who knew Jesus's brother James and Peter), independent Gospel traditions, and pre-literary material — the very evidence base graded in this investigation.
