# SRC-0035

# Adler & Borys 1996 — Two Types of Bureaucracy: Enabling and Coercive

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Theoretical/conceptual article: Adler, P. S., & Borys, B. (1996), "Two Types of Bureaucracy: Enabling and Coercive," *Administrative Science Quarterly* 41(1):61–89. It reconciles the contradictory verdicts on bureaucracy (alienating vs. supportive) by arguing that **formalization is an organizational technology that can be designed for either purpose**. It is the primary anchor for the claim that *design orientation*, not *amount*, of formalization determines its effect.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed in a top-tier organization-theory journal; widely cited (thousands of citations); Adler is a recognised authority on organizational design.
- **Transparency:** High — the argument, the borrowed "user-centered design" analogy, and the four design features are laid out explicitly and are auditable.
- **Independence:** High — academic, no commercial interest in the conclusion.
- **Intent:** Inform / theorise. It is a conceptual framework paper, not an empirical test.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It is **primarily conceptual**: the enabling/coercive typology is a *framework*, not a hypothesis tested against a dataset in this paper. Empirical support accumulated in *later* studies (e.g., management-control and accounting field studies), which this record does not itself verify.
- It concerns **workplace formalization of procedures**; extension to schemas, taxonomies, research protocols, and knowledge-architecture governance is an **analogical** transfer, not something the paper demonstrates.
- It does not quantify effect sizes or provide a decision rule for *how much* formalization is optimal.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Two types of formalization: **enabling** (procedures that give users organizational memory and help them master tasks) vs **coercive** (procedures that force compliance and effort).
- The enabling/coercive character is distinguished along **four design features**: **repair** (can users fix and improve the process, or must they follow it rigidly?), **internal transparency** (is the rule's rationale/logic visible to users?), **global transparency** (do users understand the wider system their task sits in?), and **flexibility** (how much discretion users have). *(Four features verified this session via secondary scholarly summaries; the ASQ full-text PDF was located but not machine-readable this session.)*
- Central thesis: the **same degree** of formalization can be experienced as enabling or coercive depending on these design choices — reconciling the "bureaucracy alienates" and "bureaucracy supports" positions.
