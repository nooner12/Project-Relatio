# SRC-0083

# Simard et al. 1997 — "Net Transfer of Carbon Between Ectomycorrhizal Tree Species in the Field"

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Suzanne W. Simard, David A. Perry, Melanie D. Jones, David D. Myrold, Daniel M. Durall & Randy Molina, "Net transfer of carbon between ectomycorrhizal tree species in the field," *Nature* 388(6642):579–582 (1997). The landmark field study — reciprocal isotope (¹³C/¹⁴C) labelling of paper birch and Douglas-fir — reporting **bidirectional belowground carbon transfer with a net flux to the shaded species**, regulated by source–sink relations. **In this KB it is the pro-transfer (enthusiast-pole) anchor for CLM-0063** and the paper around which the popular "wood-wide web" grew. A *News & Views* on this issue coined "wood-wide web."

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High as a peer-reviewed *Nature* field study by established forest ecologists; foundational and heavily cited.
- **Transparency:** Good on method (dual-isotope reciprocal labelling); the **inference that transfer is network-mediated rather than via soil/root pathways is where interpretation is contested** (Karst et al., SRC-0084).
- **Independence:** Simard later became the leading public advocate of the "mother tree"/wood-wide-web thesis — a motivated-reasoning vector on the *strong* downstream claims (kin provisioning, forest-wide signalling), though this 1997 paper is a measured primary result.
- **Intent:** To test whether net carbon transfer between trees occurs in the field.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Establishes **net carbon transfer occurred between two trees in one field system** (net gain to Douglas-fir ≈ 6% of its isotope uptake); does **NOT** establish that transfer is (i) ecologically significant for recipient growth, (ii) generally network-mediated across forests, or (iii) directed/adaptive ("mother trees feeding kin"). Those stronger claims are the disputed ones (CLM-0063).
- A single system, two species; generalization is exactly what Karst et al. contest.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Reciprocal isotope labelling showed **bidirectional C transfer**, with a **net flux to the more-shaded species**, consistent with **source–sink regulation.**
- Net gain to *Pseudotsuga menziesii* averaged ~6% of C acquired via photosynthesis; transfer attributed primarily to a **direct hyphal pathway.**
