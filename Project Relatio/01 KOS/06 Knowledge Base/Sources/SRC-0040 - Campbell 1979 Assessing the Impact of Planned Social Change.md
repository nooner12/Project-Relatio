# SRC-0040

# Campbell 1979 — Assessing the Impact of Planned Social Change (Campbell's Law)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Campbell, D. T. (1979), "Assessing the Impact of Planned Social Change," *Evaluation and Program Planning* 2(1):67–90. The article in which Campbell states what is now called **Campbell's Law**. It anchors the **metric-gaming / goal-displacement** failure mode: when a quantitative indicator or formal rule is turned into the target of social control, it comes under corruption pressure and distorts the very process it was meant to monitor.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — Campbell is a foundational figure in social-science methodology and quasi-experimental design; the article is widely cited and the "law" is broadly corroborated across domains.
- **Transparency:** High — the claim is stated plainly and illustrated with examples (test scores, crime statistics, etc.).
- **Independence:** High — academic.
- **Intent:** Inform / caution program evaluators.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Campbell's Law is a **generalisation / regularity**, not a quantified law with an effect size; it states a *tendency* ("the more … the more apt"), not a deterministic outcome.
- The 1979 article's evidence is **illustrative** (examples), not a systematic measurement of gaming rates. Later empirical work (education-testing, healthcare-target studies) corroborates the pattern but is not contained here.
- It concerns **indicators used for high-stakes decision-making**; low-stakes formal metrics are less subject to the pressure, a boundary condition to respect.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Campbell's Law (verbatim sense): "The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor."
- Illustrative mechanism: actors optimise the measured proxy rather than the underlying goal, degrading both the measure's validity and the process.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0031 (constraints/metrics used as targets are gamed; goal displacement — cf. Merton's "trained incapacity"/goal displacement, noted in the claim).
