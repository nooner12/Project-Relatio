# SRC-0146

# Crone & Cook (1977) — Hagarism: The Making of the Islamic World

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification

Patricia Crone and Michael Cook, *Hagarism: The Making of the Islamic World* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1977). The most widely-discussed work of the revisionist current in early-Islamic history. Setting aside the Muslim historiographical tradition as too late and too shaped by later concerns to be used as a primary framework, the authors attempted a reconstruction of Islam's origins **from contemporary non-Muslim (chiefly Syriac, Greek, Armenian, and Hebrew) sources** — arriving at a picture of an early messianic Judeo-Arab movement out of which Islam later differentiated. **This is stated as the work's reconstruction, not as an INV-0018 finding.**

Catalogued for **INV-0018** under the **ADR-GOV-0003 pattern** (catalog only, grade nothing). Serves **coverage area (b) — revisionist scholarship**, the second of two revisionist poles (with SRC-0145). It is directly relevant to **CLM A (iii)** (revisionist re-datings) and to **CLM B** (its central proposal is a strong claim about the relationship to Judaism) — which is exactly why it must be represented **at its strongest and with its reception**, and adjudicated by evidence rather than by reputation.

**Register discipline (INV-0018 §2.1).** Catalogued **as scholarship**: neither adopted as verdict nor waved away. Its **contested standing — including the authors' own later distancing — is recorded in §3 as part of the record.**

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)

- **Authority:** High as scholarship — both authors became major figures in Islamic and Near Eastern history (Crone at the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton; Cook at Princeton); Cambridge University Press. **Institutional authority is not evidence that the thesis is correct**, and this record does not treat it as such.
- **Transparency:** High in method — the source-critical premise (privileging contemporary non-Muslim testimony) is stated openly and the argument is traceable; reviewers noted the density of the citation apparatus relative to the exposition.
- **Independence:** High — critical academic scholarship, not confessional. Note that its **reception** has at times been polemically deployed by third parties; **that deployment is not the work and is not imported here.**
- **Intent:** Radically revisionist — to test whether an origins account can be constructed independently of the Islamic tradition's own historiography.

# 3. Limitations and contested standing (what this source does NOT establish)

**Its standing is contested — including by its own authors — and that contest is part of what this record carries.** Recorded neutrally, from reception surfaces read this session:

- **Substantive methodological criticism.** The central objection recurring in the reception is that the contemporary non-Muslim sources the reconstruction rests on are **not demonstrably better informed or less tendentious** than the Muslim sources set aside — many being distant from Arabic and from the events, and written in hostile registers.
- **Authorial distancing.** Michael Cook is reported (2006) to have said that the book's central thesis was, he had come to think, mistaken and insufficiently supported by the available evidence; Patricia Crone is likewise reported to have repudiated the central thesis, revisiting several of its hypotheses in her later work (*Meccan Trade and the Rise of Islam*, 1987). **These statements are recorded from secondary and reference surfaces, not read in their originals** — a verification limit disclosed here and binding at circuit.
- **Not a consensus position, and not a dismissible one.** The reconstruction is not held by the field; the book is nonetheless historically important for having forced the source-critical question, and parts of its method (systematic use of non-Muslim contemporary testimony) are now ordinary practice. **INV-0018 reports the field's actual distribution under the consensus-asymmetry rule — lopsided if lopsided — without caricaturing this pole.**
- **Interior not read this session** (§6) — no page-level citation may be drawn from it at circuit.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used

- The non-Muslim-sources method; the messianic Judeo-Arab reconstruction of origins; the later-differentiation account of Islamic confessional identity (recorded from verification and reception surfaces; **not consumed by any claim at cataloguing**).
