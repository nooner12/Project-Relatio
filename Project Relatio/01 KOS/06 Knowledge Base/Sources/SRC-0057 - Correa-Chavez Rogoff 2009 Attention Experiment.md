# SRC-0057

# Correa-Chávez & Rogoff 2009 — Children's Attention to Interactions Directed to Others

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Correa-Chávez, M., & Rogoff, B. (2009), "Children's attention to interactions directed to others: Guatemalan Mayan and European American patterns," *Developmental Psychology* 45(3):630–641. A **controlled comparative experiment** (the strongest empirical footing in this investigation for the shared-attention mechanism). Children aged **5–11** were present but **not addressed** while another child (a sibling) was shown how to build a novel toy; **a week later** each child was unexpectedly asked to build it. Three groups of **n = 40** (total ≈ 120): Mayan children from traditional families (little maternal involvement in Western schooling), Mayan children from families with extensive Western schooling, and European American children. Citation (Dev. Psychol. 45(3):630–641) verified this session via PubMed (19413421) and ERIC (EJ838530).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed experimental study in a top developmental journal.
- **Transparency:** High — clear design, groups, and outcome (spontaneous attention while non-addressed; later unprompted reproduction).
- **Independence:** Moderate — LOPI research group, but a genuine controlled test with an out-group comparison (European American), which is what gives it evidential bite.
- **Intent:** Test whether culturally patterned "learning from observing interactions directed to others" shows up as measurable attention and learning.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **Comparative, not randomized to condition:** groups are defined by **cultural background / family Western-schooling history**, not random assignment — so the effect is **confounded with the whole cultural constellation** (language, schooling, community structure). It shows a real, patterned difference; it does **not** isolate "participation" as the sole cause (central to CLM-0042's grade and CLM-0043's caution).
- **Narrow measured competence:** attention to, and one-week reproduction of, a **specific toy-construction demonstration** — a laboratory analogue of everyday observational learning, not academic achievement or broad "competence."
- **Requires another learner to observe:** the paradigm depends on an *interaction directed to another child* — precisely the substrate a **single-child home lacks** (this source `contrasts_with` CLM-0043 and is its sharpest limiting evidence).
- **Funding:** not stated/verified this session.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Non-addressed **Mayan-traditional** children showed **more sustained attention and more later learning** than Mayan-schooled or European American children.
- The effect tracks **family experience with Western schooling** — evidence that the attention style is *culturally cultivated*, and evidence of the confound.
- Design detail (learning from an interaction **directed to another**) — the load-bearing fact for the single-child-home transfer limit.
