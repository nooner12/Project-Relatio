# SRC-0060

# Angold, Costello & Worthman 1998 — Puberty and Depression: Age, Pubertal Status, and Pubertal Timing

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Angold, A., Costello, E. J., & Worthman, C. M. (1998), "Puberty and depression: the roles of age, pubertal status and pubertal timing," *Psychological Medicine* 28(1):51–61 (PubMed 9483683). A community-epidemiological study (Great Smoky Mountains Study lineage) of ~1,000+ children that disentangled **chronological age** from **physically assessed pubertal status** (Tanner staging) as predictors of the emerging female-preponderant depression ratio. Citation and the central finding (ratio emerges at Tanner Stage III+, better predicted by pubertal status than by age) verified this session via PubMed 9483683.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Construct-provenance code: `SEX-MEASURED`.** This is the one source in INV-0010 that assessed a **biological maturational variable with a stated mechanism** — Tanner-staged pubertal status, physically classified — and attributed the timing of divergence to it rather than to chronological age. It therefore **can** support a sex/biology-attributed claim under the operative rule (§3.1), with the important caveat in §3 below that "pubertal status predicts" does not by itself settle *why* (hormonal vs socially-mediated pathways). Justification: pubertal status is a physically assessed maturational construct, not a demographic label.
- **Authority:** High — landmark paper by leading developmental-epidemiology authors; heavily cited.
- **Transparency:** High — separates age, status, and timing as distinct predictors and reports which wins.
- **Independence:** High relative to INV-0010's other sources — an epidemiological cohort, not part of any advocacy program.
- **Intent:** Test whether the adolescent female depression preponderance is an age phenomenon or a puberty phenomenon.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **"Pubertal status predicts" is not "hormones cause."** Tanner stage is a physical maturational marker; it can act through hormonal pathways OR through socially-mediated ones (changed body, changed peer/adult treatment, menarche-linked social meaning). This source shows the **timing tracks a biological maturational variable**; it does **not** isolate a hormonal mechanism from a socially-mediated one. Later work (e.g., puberty-and-peer-stress mediation studies) shows social mediation is substantial — so even this `SEX-MEASURED` source only warrants a **bounded** biological-timing claim, not a clean biological-causation claim.
- **Measuring puberty is not measuring the between-sex effect (Critical Review – RQ-0010 F-2).** Puberty occurs in *both* sexes; assessing pubertal stage establishes that the *timing* of the divergence co-varies with a maturational variable, but it does **not** establish that the *between-sex* difference in depression is biologically caused — the **sex × puberty interaction was not the measured quantity.** The `SEX-MEASURED` code is correct (a biological maturational variable was physically assessed), but it licenses only a timing-co-variation claim, not a between-sex biological-causation claim.
- **Single-region cohort.** Predominantly one U.S. region; generalization is an assumption.
- **Cross-sectional pubertal linkage.** The status–depression association is strong but the design does not fully rule out reverse or third-variable effects at the individual level.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Girls exceeded boys in depression **only after the transition to mid-puberty (Tanner Stage III+)**, independent of the age at which that stage was reached.
- **Pubertal status better predicted the emergence of the sex ratio than did chronological age.**
- Pubertal **timing** (early/late) had comparatively little effect on the emergence of the ratio.
