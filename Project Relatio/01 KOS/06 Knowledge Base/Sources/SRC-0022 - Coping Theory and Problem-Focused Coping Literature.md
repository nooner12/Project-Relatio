# SRC-0022

# Coping Theory & Problem-Focused Coping Literature (Cluster)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
The transactional model of stress and coping (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984) and its contemporary descendants, distinguishing **problem-focused coping** (acting to change the stressor) from **emotion-focused coping** (regulating the response), and the **controllability-matching** finding (problem-focused coping is adaptive for controllable stressors; emotion-focused for uncontrollable ones).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High for the theory (foundational, field-defining); Moderate for the strength of the *outcome* evidence.
- **Transparency:** Mixed — a large, heterogeneous correlational literature.
- **Independence:** High.
- **Intent:** Inform / theorise.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Much of the coping-outcome evidence is **cross-sectional and correlational** — it does not cleanly establish that teaching problem-focused coping *causes* durable stress relief.
- The theory strongly motivates the response-vs-stressor distinction but must not be over-read as proof that stressor reduction is empirically superior; the *intervention* evidence is limited (see SRC-0017, CLM-0017).
- "Controllability" is itself appraised subjectively.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- **Problem-focused coping** is generally the more adaptive long-term strategy **when the stressor is controllable**, because it removes the driver rather than only managing the reaction.
- **Coping flexibility / matching:** using emotion-focused coping in high-control situations is associated with *worse* mood; matching strategy to controllability predicts better adjustment.
- Avoidance-based emotion-focused coping is associated with worse health outcomes.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0017 (the conceptual case for stressor reduction / problem-focused coping).
- `related_to` SRC-0017 (the empirical, and weaker, organisational counterpart).
