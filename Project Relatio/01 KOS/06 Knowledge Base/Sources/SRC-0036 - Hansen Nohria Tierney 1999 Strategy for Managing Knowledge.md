# SRC-0036

# Hansen, Nohria & Tierney 1999 — What's Your Strategy for Managing Knowledge?

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Practitioner/strategy article: Hansen, M. T., Nohria, N., & Tierney, T. (1999), "What's Your Strategy for Managing Knowledge?", *Harvard Business Review* 77(2), March–April 1999 (pp. 106–116). Based on study of management-consulting and other professional-service firms. It is the anchor for the **codification vs. personalization** distinction in knowledge management: codifying knowledge into formal, reusable structures is one of two strategies, each with a distinct value logic and cost.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Moderate-to-High — HBR is a leading practitioner outlet (not a peer-reviewed empirical venue); the authors are serious academics (Hansen, Nohria) plus a consulting-firm managing director (Tierney). Frequently cited as the canonical statement of the codification/personalization split.
- **Transparency:** Moderate — the underlying firm cases are described but the data and methods are not reported to research-article standard (it is a managerial essay).
- **Independence:** Moderate — one author was a senior figure at Bain & Company; the piece is broadly consultancy-facing, though the argument cuts against over-codifying.
- **Intent:** Inform / advise practitioners. Persuasive in register.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **Not an empirical test** with measured outcomes; it is a strategy framework drawn from case observation. It does not establish causal effect sizes.
- Its evidence base is **professional-service firms** (consulting); generalisation to other evolving knowledge systems is by analogy.
- It does **not** claim codification is bad — its claim is a *contingency/tradeoff*: firms that excelled picked one strategy as dominant rather than doing both equally. The "over-codification strips context" caveat is drawn additionally from the tacit-knowledge tradition (Polanyi/Nonaka), not proven here.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Two KM strategies: **codification** (knowledge extracted from the person, made person-independent, stored, and reused via databases — an "economics of reuse") and **personalization** (knowledge stays tacit and is shared person-to-person, computers used mainly to connect people).
- Codification suits **standardized, reusable** problems; personalization suits **novel, ambiguous** problems requiring deep expertise.
- Effective firms **emphasised one** strategy (roughly an 80/20 split) and used the other in support; trying to do both equally ("straddling") tended to fail.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0029 (codification's value and its tradeoff against tacit/personalized knowledge).
