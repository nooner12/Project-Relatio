# SRC-0069

# Cohn 1993 — Cosmos, Chaos and the World to Come: The Ancient Roots of Apocalyptic Faith

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Norman Cohn, *Cosmos, Chaos and the World to Come: The Ancient Roots of Apocalyptic Faith* (New Haven: Yale University Press, 1993; revised 2nd ed. 2001, with the final Zoroastrian chapter rewritten and extended). Cohn traces the apocalyptic worldview — a periodized cosmic combat culminating in the good's decisive victory, "the making wonderful," and bodily resurrection — to a Zoroastrian origin, and argues that this schema entered Jewish thought in and after the Persian period and thence the earliest Christian imagination. **In this KB it is a second pro-influence anchor**, complementary to Boyce but focused on the *apocalyptic schema* (periodized time + cosmic dualism + renovation) rather than institutional history.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction/synthesis).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High as a historian of apocalyptic/millenarian thought (Cohn's *Pursuit of the Millennium* is a classic); **not** a primary Iranologist — he relies on Iranological scholarship (incl. Boyce) for the Avestan/Pahlavi dating.
- **Transparency:** High as narrative synthesis; **the dating dependency is a weakness** — Cohn's Zoroastrian-priority argument inherits Boyce's early-dating assumptions and is exposed to the same critique.
- **Independence:** Non-confessional; but firmly on the pro-influence pole and building a single grand narrative, which invites over-smoothing of the evidence.
- **Intent:** To locate the origin of the apocalyptic faith and follow its transmission.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Its Zoroastrian-priority claim is **only as secure as the Iranological dating it borrows** — it does not independently solve the dating problem (CLM-0056).
- The grand-narrative form tends to present a contested transmission as a settled pipeline; the internal-development alternative is under-weighted.
- Cohn dates Zoroaster very early (~1200 BCE); this early date is itself a reconstruction (cf. discrimination-sample #2, H-e) and not universally held.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The apocalyptic schema (periodized combat → final victory → renovation → resurrection) is distinctively Zoroastrian in origin.
- It entered Jewish thought after the Persian conquest and underlies later Jewish apocalyptic and, downstream, Christian eschatology.
