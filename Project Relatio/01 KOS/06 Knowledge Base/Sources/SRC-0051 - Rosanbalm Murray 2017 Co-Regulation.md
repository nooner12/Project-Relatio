# Rosanbalm & Murray 2017 — Co-Regulation From Birth Through Young Adulthood

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Rosanbalm, K. D., & Murray, D. W. (2017), *Co-Regulation From Birth Through Young Adulthood: A Practice Brief*, OPRE Report #2017-79, Washington DC: Office of Planning, Research, and Evaluation (ACF, U.S. DHHS), prepared by the Frank Porter Graham Child Development Institute. A **literature-synthesis practice brief** defining co-regulation and its caregiver supports. Anchor for domain 5 and definitional source for ENT-0006. Verified this session via the ACF/OPRE and FPG publication pages and the full PDF.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Moderate–High — a US federal agency (OPRE/ACF) brief authored by a respected child-development institute (FPG); synthesises developmental-science literature. Not itself primary experimental research.
- **Transparency:** High — the three co-regulation components and the developmental framing are laid out explicitly with citations.
- **Independence:** High — government/academic, program-neutral.
- **Intent:** Translate self-regulation science for practitioners and policymakers.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It is a **synthesis / practice brief**, not a meta-analysis or trial; it does not supply effect sizes and does not itself test a program.
- The strongest evidence base is **caregiver–child (parenting)** developmental research; applying the same co-regulation supports to a **community skills program with non-parental adults** is a reasonable but **untested extension**.
- Parts of the wider self-regulation/trauma literature it draws on derive from **clinical or at-risk populations**; the brief frames co-regulation as *universal*, but any claim resting on the clinical strands inherits that provenance.
- **Funding:** U.S. federal (ACF/OPRE) — stated in the report.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Self-regulation develops through **co-regulation**: an interactive process in which caregivers (parents, teachers, coaches, mentors) support regulation across development.
- Three caregiver supports: (1) a **warm, responsive relationship**; (2) a **structured environment** — consistent, predictable routines, clear expectations, defined consequences; (3) **skills instruction/coaching** — modelling, practice, prompting, reinforcement.
- Co-regulation works through **social cues, predictability, and a relationship experienced as safe** — the "felt safety / predictability" design features of domain 5.
