# SRC-0050

# Multon, Brown & Lent 1991 — Self-Efficacy and Academic Outcomes (Meta-Analysis)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Multon, K. D., Brown, S. D., & Lent, R. W. (1991), "Relation of Self-Efficacy Beliefs to Academic Outcomes: A Meta-Analytic Investigation," *Journal of Counseling Psychology* 38(1):30–38. A **meta-analysis (~36–70 studies, per secondary summaries)** of the association between academic self-efficacy and both **performance** and **persistence**. The empirical anchor for domain 3, standing on **Bandura's (1977, 1997) social-cognitive theory**, in which **mastery (enactive) experience is the most influential of the four sources of self-efficacy.** Citation verified this session via ERIC (EJ426706), Semantic Scholar, and the DOI record.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — a foundational, heavily cited meta-analysis in a peer-reviewed journal; Bandura's theory is among the most validated frameworks in psychology.
- **Transparency:** High — meta-analytic methods and moderators reported.
- **Independence:** High — academic; independent of any program.
- **Intent:** Quantify the self-efficacy–achievement relationship.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Establishes a **correlational** relationship (self-efficacy ↔ achievement/persistence); it does **not** by itself prove that a *program* raising mastery experiences will raise achievement — that is the applied inference, weaker than the association.
- The relationship is **reciprocal** (achievement also raises efficacy); direction is not cleanly one-way.
- I could **not re-derive the exact correlations to the decimal** this session; the widely cited figures — **self-efficacy accounting for ~14% of variance in performance (r ≈ .38) and ~12% in persistence (r ≈ .34)** — are reported as **approximate**, verified only at "moderate and significant," with the 14% figure taken from a secondary summary.
- Population skews to **students in academic settings**; transfer to heritage-skill/craft mastery in children is analogical.
- **Funding:** not applicable/not stated (secondary analysis of published studies).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Positive, statistically significant relationships of self-efficacy with **academic performance** and **persistence** across subjects, designs, and measures.
- Self-efficacy accounted for **~14% of variance** in academic performance — large for a single psychological predictor (reported as approximate).
- Theoretical frame: **mastery experience is the strongest source of self-efficacy** (Bandura), the mechanism a skills-mastery program would target.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0036 (mastery → self-efficacy → persistence/achievement).
