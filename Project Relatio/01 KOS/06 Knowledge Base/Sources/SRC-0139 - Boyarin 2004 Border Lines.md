# SRC-0139

# Boyarin (2004) — Border Lines: The Partition of Judaeo-Christianity

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Daniel Boyarin, *Border Lines: The Partition of Judaeo-Christianity* (Divinations: Rereading Late Ancient Religion; Philadelphia: University of Pennsylvania Press, 2004; xviii + 374 pp.). A major statement of the **"late and constructed" reading** of the Jewish–Christian separation: Boyarin argues the border between the two was **drawn late, from above, largely through heresiological work**, rather than reflecting an early or natural divergence. Directly load-bearing for **INV-0017's descent edges and its rival-reading element** — it is one pole of the early-vs-late / gradual-vs-sharp debate the investigation must adjudicate. Catalogued for INV-0017 (ADR-GOV-0003 pattern); **no claim asserted at cataloguing**, and its thesis is recorded as a *position in the literature*, not an INV-0017 finding.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — Boyarin is a leading scholar of rabbinics and of Jewish–Christian relations in late antiquity; University of Pennsylvania Press, in a peer series (Divinations).
- **Transparency:** High — an explicit, heavily argued revisionist thesis with its sources on display.
- **Independence:** High — critical academic scholarship.
- **Intent:** Revisionist — to dissolve the assumption of two pre-formed religions "parting," and to relocate the boundary-making in later heresiology and rabbinic self-definition.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **A strong thesis, not a neutral survey** — it advances one contested position; the opposing "earlier / relatively clean partings" reading (SRC-0140, Dunn) is a live scholarly alternative. INV-0017 must weigh both, not inherit either from the catalog.
- Its focus is the *boundary-drawing* process, not primarily the emergence-dating of Second Temple Judaism as such (Area 1 sources cover that).
- **Interior not read this session** (see §6).

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The "border lines drawn late, via heresiology" thesis and the critique of the "parting of the ways" model (recorded from verification surfaces; not consumed by any claim at cataloguing).
