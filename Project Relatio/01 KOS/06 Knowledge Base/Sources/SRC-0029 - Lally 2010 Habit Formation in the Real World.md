# SRC-0029

# Lally et al. 2010 — How Are Habits Formed: Modelling Habit Formation in the Real World

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Prospective real-world study of habit (automaticity) formation: 96 participants chose a daily eating, drinking, or activity behaviour to perform in a consistent context for 12 weeks, self-rating automaticity: Lally, van Jaarsveld, Potts & Wardle (2010), *European Journal of Social Psychology* 40(6):998–1009. It grounds the habit-formation route to maintenance and corrects the "21-day habit" myth.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed, widely cited foundational habit-formation study.
- **Transparency:** High — modelling approach and individual variation reported.
- **Independence:** High — academic.
- **Intent:** Inform.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- **Small sample (N=96)** and self-report automaticity; the 66-day median is an estimate with a very wide range, not a precise universal constant.
- Simple behaviours in a single self-chosen context; complex/effortful wellness behaviours (structured exercise, dietary overhaul) may automate more slowly or incompletely.
- Automaticity ≠ guaranteed long-term maintenance; it is one supporting mechanism.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Median time to reach ~95% of asymptotic automaticity was **≈66 days**, with a wide range (**≈18–254 days**) — substantial individual and behaviour-type variation. *(Verified at authoring via PMC, Wiley, and University of Surrey records; **re-verified 2026-07-11** (remediation pass) via Wiley/EJSP and independent summaries — "median 66 days … range 18 to 254 days" confirmed.)*
- Missing a single opportunity did **not** materially impair habit formation — occasional lapses are tolerable.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0025 (habit formation supports maintenance but is slower/more variable than popularly claimed).
