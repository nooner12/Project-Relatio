# SRC-0047

# Kaleta et al. 2025 — Nature-Based Interventions: A Systematic Review of Reviews

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Kaleta, B., Campbell, S., O'Keeffe, J., & Burke, J. (2025), "Nature-based interventions: a systematic review of reviews," *Frontiers in Psychology* 16:1625294. A **meta-synthesis of 61 peer-reviewed systematic reviews** identifying 13 categories of nature-based interventions (NBIs) and their outcomes across age groups. Anchor for domain 1 (nature). Verified this session via WebFetch of the Frontiers full text (authors, review-of-reviews design, and conclusions confirmed directly).

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** High — peer-reviewed umbrella review of 61 systematic reviews; the highest tier of synthesis available for this domain.
- **Transparency:** High — states its own quality appraisal (only 16/61 reviews met quality standards for moderator/mediator analysis; only 7 explored such factors).
- **Independence:** Moderate–High — academic; nature-connectedness/wellbeing field carries some advocacy energy, partly offset by the authors' explicit quality caveats.
- **Intent:** Map and appraise the NBI evidence base.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- Documents **short-term** attentional benefits; states that **long-term** NBI benefits were "difficult to assess" — durability is not established.
- Umbrella reviews inherit the **primary studies' weaknesses**: much underlying evidence is observational, of varied quality, and heterogeneous in "nature exposure" definition.
- **English-language, peer-reviewed** restriction may miss interventions; only a minority of included reviews examined **for whom / under what conditions** effects hold.
- Does **not** isolate mentored, skills-based, or heritage-craft programming; "nature-based intervention" here spans forest schools, greenspace exposure, horticulture, adventure, etc.
- **Funding:** not verified this session.

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- Benefits of NBIs on **attentional functioning in children in the short term** documented; long-term benefits hard to assess.
- **Social interaction** through NBIs and **nature connectedness** identified as mediators of wellbeing outcomes.
- Quality caveat: only **7 of 61** reviews explored moderating/mediating factors.

# 5. Relationships (STD-0004)
- `supports` CLM-0034 (nature and attention), corroborated by the broader greenspace–mental-health review literature noted in that claim.
