# SRC-0091

# Metzger & Ehrman — The Text of the New Testament (4th ed., 2005)

## Draft Source Record

---

# 1. Identification
Bruce M. Metzger & Bart D. Ehrman, *The Text of the New Testament: Its Transmission, Corruption, and Restoration*, 4th ed. (Oxford University Press, 2005). The standard English-language handbook of New Testament textual criticism. In this KB it is the **primary secondary authority** on the manuscript base (papyri, uncials, minuscules, versions, patristic citations), the classification and count of variants, and the reach and limits of text-critical reconstruction of the wording. Metzger represents the mainstream text-critical establishment; Ehrman's co-authorship of the 4th edition adds the more skeptical accent on the theological significance of some variants.

**H-band of this source:** **H-e** (modern scholarly reconstruction) — a critical handbook, not a witness. It *evaluates* the H-b (material/papyrological) and H-a (the texts themselves) evidence; it is not itself that evidence.

# 2. Source Evaluation (KOS-0003 §6)
- **Authority:** Very high — Metzger was the dean of Anglophone NT textual criticism; this handbook is the field-standard teaching text through four editions.
- **Transparency:** High — methods (genealogical, eclectic, reasoned-eclecticism) are laid out explicitly; disputed cases are shown as disputed.
- **Independence:** High — the two authors span the establishment (Metzger) and the skeptical-popularizing (Ehrman) poles, which checks confessional slippage in either direction.
- **Intent:** To inform and to train practitioners; not apologetic and not polemical.

# 3. Limitations (what this source does NOT establish)
- It establishes how *recoverably the wording* is transmitted; it establishes **nothing** about whether the *content* of the texts is historically true.
- Text-critical reconstruction targets the earliest *recoverable* text, not a demonstrably identical *autograph* — the step from best-attested reading to original wording is itself reasoned reconstruction (H-e), with a residual, bounded uncertainty.
- The famous variant-count figures (commonly cited as c. 300,000–400,000 variant readings across the tradition) are counts of *all* differences, the overwhelming majority orthographic/trivial; the raw number overstates substantive uncertainty and must not be read as "400,000 errors."

# 4. Key Content / Passages Used
- The NT is attested by an unusually rich manuscript base (thousands of Greek manuscripts, plus versions and patristic citations) — far denser than any other ancient text.
- Metzger & Ehrman's own conclusion: were all continuous-text manuscripts lost, the patristic quotations alone would suffice to reconstruct practically the entire NT — a statement of *recoverability*, not inerrancy.
- The overwhelming majority of variants are insignificant (spelling, word order); a small set are substantive and text-critically contested (e.g. the longer ending of Mark 16:9–20; the *pericope adulterae* John 7:53–8:11; 1 John 5:7–8 Comma Johanneum), which bound the "perfectly preserved" claim without vindicating "hopelessly corrupt."
