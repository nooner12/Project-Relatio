# 1. Research Question

**Original (verbatim, owner):** "What are the most common factors that lead to avoidance or failure to maintain wellness interventions, and the strongest evidence for effective interventions for these avoidances?"

**Two-part structure (as posed):** (a) the most common factors behind non-adoption / non-adherence / non-maintenance / relapse of wellness interventions; and (b) the interventions with the strongest evidence for counteracting those factors (improving adherence and long-term maintenance).

**Refined (Specialist):** Retained as a two-part question, with two **clarifying** (not redirecting) scope decisions stated explicitly: (i) **"wellness interventions"** is read as **self-initiated / lifestyle health-behaviour interventions** — exercise, dietary change, stress-reduction and mind-body practices, sleep — per the owner's scope guidance, drawing on the **behavioural-medicine adherence, dropout, and behaviour-change-maintenance** literature; and (ii) an explicit **framing boundary** (§2): the review answers the *general evidence question*, not any individual's or client's case.

*Rationale for the refinement.* The question is already well-scoped; the only additions are (1) fixing "wellness interventions" to the lifestyle-health-behaviour sense the owner named, and (2) the not-advice boundary standard for this domain. Neither **materially redirects or narrows** what was asked (ROLE-0002 §4.2a) — the broad cross-cutting treatment the owner explicitly authorised is delivered in full — so no Steward pre-confirmation was required. The scope reading is nonetheless surfaced here and in the review brief so the owner/reviewer can catch it.

# 2. Scope & Disambiguation — including the not-advice and safety framing

**In scope:**
- **Part (a) — factors behind failure:** the intention–behaviour gap; motivation quality (autonomous vs controlled) and self-efficacy; the distinct determinants of *maintenance* vs *initiation* (habit, self-regulation, resources, environment); practical/contextual barriers and life disruption; and early attrition in self-directed/digital formats.
- **Part (b) — best-evidenced interventions:** self-monitoring and self-regulation techniques; implementation intentions / action-and-coping planning; autonomy-supportive motivation building; habit formation via context-cued repetition; and financial incentives (assessed as a *bounded/cautionary* case).
- Evidence base: systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and landmark studies in health/behavioural psychology.

**Out of scope / bracketed:**
- **Individualized advice.** The review does not tell any specific person or client how to change or maintain a behaviour.
- **Medication adherence as a clinical-pharmacology topic** (distinct literature) — touched only where general behaviour-change principles overlap.
- **Clinical treatment** of diagnosed disorders (eating disorders, clinical depression/anxiety, addiction) — professional help is warranted and not substituted for here.
- **Exhaustive per-behaviour barrier catalogues** (e.g. every named barrier to exercise); the investigation treats the *cross-cutting* factors and best-evidenced remedies, which is the broad treatment the owner authorised.

**Safety framing (load-bearing):**
1. **Educational, general evidence only** — not a recommendation for any individual.
2. **Effects are modest and durability evidence is thin** (see FND-0006 §3) — the review deliberately does not promise adherence.
3. **Professional help is warranted** where non-adherence reflects a clinical condition or where a wellness change interacts with a medical condition/medication.

**Disambiguation:**
- **"Adherence / maintenance / relapse":** *adherence* = doing the behaviour as intended over the intervention period; *maintenance* = continuing after the active intervention ends (the harder, less-evidenced endpoint); *relapse* = returning to the prior behaviour. The investigation grades **short-to-medium-term** and **long-term (>12-month)** evidence separately in spirit, following the INV-0005 durability precedent.
- **"Wellness intervention"** here = lifestyle health-behaviour change, not a clinical/pharmacological treatment.

# 4. Findings / Synthesis

See **[[FND-0006 - What Best Supports Sustained Wellness Behaviour]]**. In brief:

**(a) Why maintenance fails.** The core failure is the **intention–behaviour gap** — intention is a weak cause of action, and roughly half of intenders fail to act (CLM-0019). It is widened by **low-quality (controlled) motivation and low self-efficacy** (CLM-0020), by the fact that **maintenance is governed by different determinants than initiation** — habit, self-regulation, resources, environment (CLM-0021) — and, in self-directed/digital formats, by **pervasive early attrition** (CLM-0022).

**(b) What best counteracts it.** The best-evidenced levers automate and translate behaviour rather than exhort it: **self-monitoring + self-regulation** (strongest ingredient, small-to-moderate effect; CLM-0023), **implementation intentions** (most direct gap-closer, medium-to-large proximal effect; CLM-0024), **autonomy-supportive motivation** (CLM-0020), and **habit formation** via context-cued repetition (slower than the "21-day" myth; CLM-0025). **Financial incentives** work only while in place (CLM-0026) — the instructive case of what does *not* durably work.

**Bottom line.** There is **no reliable formula** that makes people stick. The defensible strategy is a **combination** — self-regulation skills + autonomous motivation + deliberate habit-building — with the honest caveat that effects are modest and **long-term maintenance evidence is thin across the board**, echoing INV-0004 and INV-0005.

# 6. Assumptions & Bracketing (KOS-0003 §10)

- **Motivated-reasoning guard (named, per instruction).** The owner runs a wellness business; a confident "here is how to make clients stick with wellness programmes" conclusion would be **commercially convenient**. The evidence was read *against* that prior and the convenient conclusion **did not fully survive**: (i) there is **no reliable adherence formula**; (ii) even the best-evidenced techniques have **modest effects** and **thin long-term durability evidence**; (iii) the lever closest to a "sell motivation / reward engagement" model — **external financial incentives — is exactly the one that fails after removal** (CLM-0026). Symmetrically, the finding congenial to an "align, don't force" brand (autonomy-supportive motivation, CLM-0020) was **graded down to Moderate** for its correlational base rather than inflated. The answer is stated only in its qualified form.
- **Methodological:** the strongest evidence anchors are exercise and eating behaviour; extension to meditation, breathwork, and sleep is partly inferential. Techniques are studied mostly for **initiation and short-to-medium-term** outcomes; long-term maintenance is under-evidenced, and absence of evidence is treated as **uncertainty**, not disproof.
- **Single-source legs:** CLM-0021 (Kwasnicka), CLM-0022 (Eysenbach), CLM-0025 (Lally), and CLM-0026 (Mantzari) each rest substantially on one authoritative source; graded accordingly (Moderate where the source is a theory-synthesis or conceptual/single-study).
- **Verification:** all nine sources were live-verified this session; the one figure that resisted precise pinning (Rhodes intender-failure %) is reported as "approximately half," not a decimal (§12.1). No figure was invented.
- **Bracketed as out of scope:** the individual/client case; clinical treatment of diagnosed disorders; pharmacological medication-adherence; exhaustive per-behaviour barrier lists.
