# CLM-0022

# In Self-Directed and Digital Wellness Interventions, Early Attrition Is the Norm

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> In **self-directed and digital** wellness interventions (apps, online programmes, wearables, self-guided courses), **substantial early disengagement is the baseline expectation, not the exception** — the "law of attrition." A large share of users stop using the tool ("non-usage attrition") well before any dose sufficient for benefit, independent of how efficacious the intervention is when actually used. Reported efficacy therefore routinely **overstates real-world adherence**.

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Descriptive** (a characteristic empirical pattern of digital/self-directed interventions).

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Conceptual framing (2005) plus modern objective corroboration (2019).**
- **SRC-0032 (Eysenbach 2005, "The Law of Attrition"):** in eHealth applications a substantial proportion of users drop out or stop use before completion; distinguishes **non-usage attrition** from **dropout attrition**; notes high dropout is often under-reported because researchers treat it as failure.
- **SRC-0034 (Baumel et al. 2019):** panel-based analysis of **objective** real-world usage of 93 mental-health/well-being apps found retention very low and front-loaded — median 15-day retention ≈3.9% and 30-day retention ≈3.3% (wide IQRs). This is contemporary, objective (non-self-report) corroboration that steep early disengagement is characteristic of the *current* app ecosystem, updating the dated 2005 viewpoint. (Reported as one benchmark with wide variability, not a universal rate.)

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- **Reliability: 3** — the 2005 framing is a **viewpoint/editorial**, but it is now paired with a peer-reviewed 2019 objective-usage study (SRC-0034); the *phenomenon* is well supported, though neither source is a pooled meta-analysis of attrition *rates*.
- **Relevance: 4** — directly names why self-directed wellness tools fail to be *maintained* (the delivery mode most relevant to a modern wellness business).
- **Independence: 3** — now two independent sources of different kind and era (a 2005 conceptual framing and a 2019 objective usage panel), which addresses the earlier single-dated-source concern; still not a systematic meta-analysis of attrition magnitudes.
- **Quality: 3** — strong conceptual contribution plus one modern objective benchmark; no pooled quantitative estimate across the field.
- **Limitations:** Establishes that early attrition is **pervasive and characteristic**; it does **not** provide a single precise, field-wide attrition rate, nor fully resolve its causes. No universal attrition percentage is asserted (KOS-0003 §12.1); the Baumel figures are reported as one benchmark with wide variability.

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- Evidence strength: **Moderate** (well-observed pattern; specific magnitudes vary widely by study).
- Consensus strength: **High** — that digital-health attrition is high and characteristic is broadly accepted.
- Relationship: the phenomenon is agreed; exact rates and remedies are study-specific.

---

# Source Evaluation
SRC-0032 (Eysenbach 2005): high authority as the originating concept, but conceptual not quantitative; verified via JMIR/PMC. SRC-0034 (Baumel 2019): peer-reviewed objective-usage benchmark verified this remediation session via JMIR/PMC-6785720, supplying modern magnitudes and mitigating the dated-single-source concern. The claim still deliberately avoids asserting a single universal attrition rate (KOS-0003 §12.1); Baumel's figures are cited as one benchmark with wide variability.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Methodological:** the 2005 pattern persists in current app/wearable ecosystems — now directly corroborated by objective 2019 usage data (SRC-0034), reducing (though not eliminating) reliance on extrapolation from the dated source.
- **Epistemological:** under-reporting of dropout means published adherence is an upper bound on real-world adherence.

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Inductive / abductive.** The pervasiveness of early disengagement across eHealth trials is best explained as a characteristic property of self-directed digital delivery, not idiosyncratic failure. **Risks checked:** *dated source* (named — magnitudes must come from current empirical work); *single-source reliance* (named — independence graded 2); *false precision* (no attrition number asserted).

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8)
**Level 3 (Moderate).** The existence and importance of high early attrition in digital/self-directed wellness is well established and directly relevant, now supported by a 2005 conceptual framing plus a 2019 objective usage benchmark. It is held at Moderate — not higher — because there is still no pooled, field-wide meta-analysis of attrition magnitudes or remedies, and the record deliberately asserts no single universal rate.

---

# Limitations
- No precise attrition rate asserted; magnitude is study-specific.
- Source predates modern app/wearable platforms.
- Attrition ≠ inefficacy of the *content*; it is an adherence/engagement problem.

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **"Attrition just means the tool is bad."** Partly — but attrition is high even for efficacious tools; it is a mode-of-delivery problem (self-direction) as much as a content problem; rejected as the whole story.
2. **"Newer apps have solved attrition."** Not supported — objective 2019 usage data (SRC-0034: median 30-day retention ≈3.3% across 93 popular apps) show engagement remains a central, unsolved challenge; rejected.
