# CLM-0037

# Structured Youth Mentoring by Non-Parental Adults Produces Small but Real Average Benefits, Conditional on Relationship Quality and Program Structure

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> Structured mentoring by a **non-parental adult** produces **small but statistically real average benefits** across behavioural, social-emotional, and academic outcomes. The effect is **conditional**: it is larger with **longer, higher-quality relationships**, **interest-based matching**, **adherence to program best practices**, and for **higher-risk youth**; poorly implemented or short-lived matches can yield null or even negative effects.

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Causal** — supported by meta-analysis (SRC-0048) and a randomized controlled trial (SRC-0049); the moderator pattern is descriptive/correlational within that causal frame.

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Empirical** (meta-analysis + RCT).
- **SRC-0048 (DuBois et al. 2011):** meta-analysis of **73 evaluations**; **overall d ≈ 0.21** ("small but significant"); effects enhanced by best-practice adherence, strong relationships, and interest-based matching (≈.41 vs ≈.20); higher-risk youth benefit most.
- **SRC-0049 (Tierney, Grossman & Resch 1995):** RCT of **959 youth** (BBBS), 18-month follow-up; **statistically significant but small** favourable effects on drug/alcohol initiation, school, fighting, and relationship quality.
- **Design/population:** children/adolescents ≤18; community mentoring programs; comparison-group and randomized designs.

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- **Reliability: 4** — meta-analysis plus a true RCT; strong for direction.
- **Relevance: 4** — the studied interventions *are* structured, adult-mentored community programs — the closest domain to the application.
- **Independence: 4** — 73 independent evaluations plus an independent RCT.
- **Quality: 4** — high design quality; the honest limitation is the **small magnitude**, not the rigour.
- **Limitations:** establishes **small average causal benefit** and its moderators; does **not** establish large effects, durability beyond follow-up windows, or that a **skills-based/heritage** mentoring model outperforms the friendship model that was studied.

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- Evidence strength: **High** that mentoring produces small positive average effects.
- Consensus strength: **High** — this is the settled reading in the mentoring field (its own leaders report the small effect candidly).
- Relationship: strongly supported, with agreed caveats about magnitude and implementation dependence.

---

# Source Evaluation
SRC-0048/SRC-0049: high authority and transparency; DuBois d ≈ 0.21 corroborated across secondary summaries (primary paywalled); BBBS design verified in the full report, the ≈0.06 SMD flagged as a secondary re-analysis figure.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Methodological:** meta-analytic moderators approximate causal design features — partly (they are correlational within the meta-analysis).
- **Cultural:** the non-parental-adult relationship functions similarly across program types — assumed, tested mainly in friendship-mentoring models.

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Deductive-within-RCT + inductive meta-analytic generalisation.** **Risks checked:** *effect-size inflation* (named — magnitude is small, held small); *moderator over-reading* (named — moderators are correlational, not proven levers); *transfer to a different mentoring model* (named — heritage-skills mentoring is not the studied model, quarantined into the application grade).

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8) — DUAL GRADE
- **Underlying-evidence confidence: Level 4 (High)** — that structured mentoring yields small-but-real, implementation-dependent benefits. Not Level 5: effects are small, heterogeneous, and durability is limited.
- **Application confidence (generic community heritage-skills program): Level 2 (reaching toward 3) (Low–Moderate)** — **the closest match in the whole investigation**: the studied interventions are structured community programs with non-parental adults. It is held below High because (a) the studied model is friendship mentoring, not skills/heritage mentoring; (b) average effects are small and implementation-sensitive; (c) benefit depends on relationship duration/quality that a program must actually achieve, not merely intend.

---

# Limitations
- Small average magnitude; large effects are not supported.
- Durability beyond follow-up windows unestablished.
- Moderators are correlational; implementing them does not guarantee the effect.

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **"Mentoring effects are so small they are practically negligible."** Partly conceded on magnitude — but a small, reliable, low-cost effect across many outcomes is still meaningful; refined, not rejected.
2. **"Skills-based mentoring should beat friendship mentoring."** Plausible steelman (shared activity may deepen the relationship DuBois found to matter) — but **unproven**; it raises, not lowers, the value of the moderator evidence while keeping the application grade honest.
