# 1. Research Question

**Original (verbatim):** "What does the research literature support regarding structured, adult-mentored, skills-based programming (heritage skills, outdoor/nature settings, hands-on craft) as a context for healthy child development — and at what confidence, per outcome domain?"

**Refined (Specialist):** Retained as posed. The question is decomposed into **six evidence domains** (below) so that each strand of the proposed program is graded against *its own* literature rather than the compound being graded as a whole. A **dual-grading discipline** is applied throughout: (1) **underlying-evidence confidence** — what each literature itself supports; and (2) **application confidence** — how much that transfers to the generic proposed application: *structured, adult-mentored, family-context, heritage-skills programming for children delivered by community organizations.*

*Rationale for the refinement.* This is a **clarifying decomposition, not a redirection** (ROLE-0002 §4.2a). It does not bracket any part of what was asked; it makes the per-domain confidence request in the question operational and forces the honesty step the question itself demands ("at what confidence, per outcome domain"). No Steward pre-confirmation was required; the scope reading is surfaced here and in the review brief.

# 2. Scope & Disambiguation

**Six evidence domains** (the pull is structured around these):
1. **Outdoor / nature-based programming** — attentional, emotional-regulation, and mental-wellbeing outcomes of nature exposure and nature-based interventions.
2. **Risky play and graduated physical challenge** — physical activity, social health, injury, and (more weakly) self-regulation / anxiety outcomes.
3. **Skill mastery / competence experiences** — Bandura's mastery-experience pathway to self-efficacy, and self-efficacy's association with persistence and achievement.
4. **Non-parental adult mentorship** — structured mentoring-program outcomes and their moderators.
5. **Trauma-aware program-design features** — predictability, routine, co-regulation through skilled adult presence, graduated challenge, felt safety — treated as **universal program-design features, not interventions for identified populations**; clinical provenance is flagged where it applies.
6. **Intergenerational skill transmission / family ritual** — family routines and rituals and their association with child adjustment.

**"Structured, adult-mentored, skills-based programming"** — a recurring, adult-led activity in which children build a concrete skill (craft, heritage, outdoor) under a warm, competent, non-parental adult, with predictable routine and graduated challenge. This is the **generic application target** against which the second (application) grade is set.

**In scope:** what each of the six literatures supports, and the honest transfer distance to the compound application.
**Out of scope / bracketed:**
- **Individualized clinical or developmental advice** — no claim addresses a specific child, diagnosis, or family (CLAUDE.md / ADR-GOV-0002; the gate above).
- **A direct trial of the compound program.** No located study evaluates the specific bundle (heritage-skills + nature setting + mentoring + family ritual) as one intervention; this absence is itself a finding (CLM-0040).
- **Exhaustive survey** of any single domain — each is anchored on one or two strong, representative sources, not a full meta-review.
- **Causal certification of magnitude** — most domains offer directional/associational evidence and few clean effect sizes; none were invented (KOS-0003 §12.1).

# 3. Method

Followed the KOS-0003 pipeline (Question → Claims → Assumptions → Evidence → Confidence). Seven sources were located and **live-verified this session**. Each domain gets one claim; a seventh integrative claim (CLM-0040) records the compound-intervention evidence gap. Every claim carries the mandated **dual grade** (underlying-evidence + application) plus study design, population, effect direction/rough magnitude, funding note, and flags for self-report / small-trial / correlational / clinical-provenance evidence.

One entity (**ENT-0006 Co-Regulation**) is modelled because it is load-bearing across domains 4 and 5 and is a plausibly cross-investigation developmental construct.

**§12.1 governs all quantitative handling:** the two hardest numbers reported — DuBois et al.'s overall mentoring effect (**d ≈ 0.21**) and Multon et al.'s self-efficacy–achievement association (**~14% of variance**) — are reported at verified precision; figures I could not re-derive to the decimal this session (e.g., Multon's exact per-outcome correlations; Fiese's page range) are flagged as approximate, not asserted.

# 4. Findings / Synthesis

See **[[FND-0008 - Skills Programming Evidence]]**. In brief: the **component** literatures give real but mostly **modest, associational** support for the ingredients of such programming, with **mentoring** and **mastery/self-efficacy** the best-evidenced pathways and **family ritual** the weakest. The **compound program as a whole is untested**, so the application grade for "a community heritage-skills program improves child development outcomes" is bounded to **Level 1–2** across every domain, regardless of how strong the underlying science is. The honest headline: the ingredients are plausible and several are well-supported *in their own right*; the specific recipe is a reasonable, evidence-informed hypothesis, not a demonstrated intervention.

# 6. Assumptions & Bracketing (KOS-0003 §10)

- **Motivated-reasoning guard (named).** The convenient conclusion is that "a wholesome, hands-on, mentored, outdoor skills program is good for kids" — a conclusion that is culturally attractive and would flatter any such program. The evidence was read *against* that prior. It **partly survives** (mentoring and mastery pathways are genuinely well-evidenced) but the convenient *compound* claim does **not** survive: there is no trial of the bundle, several strands are weak (family ritual) or of clinical provenance (co-regulation), and effect sizes where they exist are **small** (mentoring d ≈ 0.21; BBBS effects small). The application grades are held at Level 1–2 deliberately, not softened upward.
- **Ecological-transfer assumption (the key limitation).** Each source studied a *specific* intervention (a greenspace program, a mentoring match, a school self-efficacy measure), not the proposed heritage-skills bundle. Transferring these to "community skills programming" is analogical; this is flagged on every claim and is the single most contestable move.
- **Provenance assumption for domain 5.** The co-regulation / felt-safety design features are strongest as *universal developmental* supports; parts of the broader trauma-informed literature derive from clinical / identified-trauma populations. Where that provenance limits general application, it is stated (CLM-0038).
- **Publication / enthusiasm bias.** The outdoor-play and nature literatures carry advocacy energy; the graded claims track what the *reviews' own quality appraisals* support (e.g., Brussoni's GRADE caveats; Kaleta's finding that only 7/61 reviews examined moderators), not the field's enthusiasm.
- **Bracketed:** any individualized advice; a direct evaluation of the compound program; validated magnitude estimates; long-term/durability outcomes (most evidence is short-term).

# 9. Review-Targeting Handoff (OPS-0003 §3.1)

**For the Critical Reviewer — spend your effort here:**

- **Weakest claims (attack first):** **CLM-0039 (family routines/rituals)** — the underlying evidence (Fiese et al. 2002) is a *narrative* review of ~32 mostly cross-sectional, correlational studies; confounding (functional families both ritualize and raise well-adjusted kids) is barely controlled. I graded it U = Level 2 / A = Level 1; check I have not been too generous. **CLM-0035 (risky play, regulation/anxiety strand)** — the physical-activity/social benefits are decently reviewed, but the *self-regulation and anxiety-reduction* claims are the field's enthusiasm running ahead of the GRADE-rated evidence; I split the grade to isolate this, but the split itself is a judgment call.
- **Most contestable judgment call:** the entire **application-grade ceiling of Level 1–2**. I held every application grade low on the argument that no study tests the compound and ecological transfer is weak. A reviewer could reasonably argue mentoring (domain 4) deserves A = Level 3 because the studied interventions *are* structured community programs with non-parental adults — quite close to the application. I gave it the benefit of the Low–Moderate band; challenge whether that is too high or too low.
- **Numbers I could NOT verify to the decimal this session (do not treat as precise):** Multon, Brown & Lent (1991) exact per-outcome correlations (I report the widely-cited **~14% of variance / r ≈ .38 for performance** as approximate, verified only at the "moderate, significant" level and the 14% figure via secondary summary); Fiese et al. (2002) **page range** (verified to *Journal of Family Psychology* 16(4), 2002; page numbers reported as commonly cited ~381–390, not confirmed to the page); Tierney et al. (1995) BBBS **SMD = 0.06** (this magnitude came from a secondary re-analysis/Blueprints summary, not the original report's own headline; I describe the effects qualitatively as "statistically significant but small" and flag the 0.06). DuBois et al. (2011) **d ≈ 0.21** was corroborated across multiple secondary summaries but I did not open the paywalled primary.
- **What I bracketed and did not settle:** the durability of any effect (most evidence short-term); whether heritage-skill/craft content specifically (as opposed to any structured skill) adds anything — no located evidence isolates *heritage* content; and any dose-response for "how much" programming.
- **Fabrication self-check:** every source above resolves to a real, locatable publication; no effect size, DOI, or figure was invented. Where I was unsure of a number I flagged it rather than asserting it.
