# CLM-0013

# Attention-Regulation Techniques Reduce Stress Short-Term but Durability Is Thin

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> Response-regulation techniques that train attention or arousal — MBSR / mindfulness meditation, relaxation, and slow-breathing / HRV biofeedback — produce **reliable small-to-moderate reductions in perceived stress and anxiety in the short-to-medium term (weeks to about a year)**, but the evidence that these effects **durably persist beyond ~1 year is thin**, the effects **attenuate over time**, and they **shrink substantially under higher-quality and active-control comparisons.**

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Causal** (the techniques reduce stress) with a **descriptive** component about the *strength and durability* of that effect. Causal claims require mechanism, evidence, alternatives, and confounding analysis — all engaged below.

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Empirical** (meta-analyses of RCTs and controlled trials).
- **SRC-0014 (Goyal 2014, JAMA Intern Med; 47 RCTs, 3,320):** mindfulness effect on anxiety **0.38 (8 wk) → 0.22 (3–6 mo)** and depression **0.30 → 0.23** — small, and **declining**; low/insufficient evidence for stress/distress, positive mood, well-being; **no significant advantage over active controls** (exercise, PMR, CBT).
- **SRC-0012 (Khoury 2015):** MBSR in healthy adults, Hedges' g ≈ **0.66 pre-post / 0.53 between-group** (moderate), maintained only at short follow-up (~19 wk average). *(Pre-post figure reconciled per Critical Review – RQ-0005 §6.4.)*
- **SRC-0013 (de Vibe 2017, Campbell):** effects maintained across a **1–34 month** range, but **~2/3 of studies at considerable risk of bias** and **higher-quality studies report lower effects.**
- **SRC-0015 (Goessl 2017; 24 studies, 484):** HRV biofeedback g ≈ **0.81/0.83** on stress/anxiety — large, but **small evidence base and no durability follow-up.**
- **SRC-0016 (Cochrane, Tamminga 2023):** individual-level "attention-toward" and "attention-away" techniques **may** reduce stress short-to-medium term, effect **potentially up to a year, on low-certainty evidence.**

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- **Reliability: 4 (short-term) / 2 (long-term)** — multiple meta-analyses agree on a short-term effect; long-term rests on low-certainty, short-follow-up data.
- **Relevance: 5** — directly measures stress/anxiety outcomes.
- **Independence: 4** — dietary of methods (mindfulness, relaxation, breathing) and independent syntheses (an AHRQ report, a Campbell review, a Cochrane review) converge.
- **Quality: 3** — pervasive risk of bias, small studies, active-control shrinkage; the most rigorous synthesis (Goyal) is the least impressed.
- **Limitations:** Establishes a **short-to-medium-term** effect. Does **not** establish durable (>1 yr) relief, superiority over other active interventions, or effects uninflated by expectancy/allegiance.

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- Evidence strength: **Moderate** (short-term), **Low** (long-term).
- Consensus strength: **High** that there is *a* short-term effect; **High** among rigorous reviewers that it is *small and comparator-dependent*; genuine uncertainty about durability.
- Relationship: the short-term effect is real but modest; durability is unproven, not disproven.

---

# Source Evaluation
Goyal (SRC-0014) is the highest-authority, most independent, anti-allegiance synthesis and is weighted most. Khoury/de Vibe (SRC-0012/0013) carry allegiance and bias caveats. Goessl (SRC-0015) is a small, promising, non-durability base. Cochrane (SRC-0016) supplies the honest low-certainty ceiling.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Methodological:** self-reported perceived-stress scales validly capture "chronic stress"; short-follow-up maintenance would extrapolate to the long term (this assumption is **not** supported — hence the low long-term grade).
- **Epistemological:** meta-analytic pooling across heterogeneous techniques/populations is informative despite heterogeneity.
- **Cultural/commercial:** the mindfulness/breathwork literature is embedded in a wellness industry with allegiance and publication-bias pressures — named explicitly (this is the owner's-business hazard from INV-0005 §6).

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Inductive.** Multiple independent meta-analyses show a convergent short-term effect that **decays** with follow-up (0.38→0.22 in Goyal) and **shrinks** with study quality and active controls. The honest inference is a real but modest short-term effect of uncertain durability. **Risks checked:** *confirmation bias / commercial motivation* (named and guarded — the convenient conclusion "these techniques durably fix stress" is the one the evidence least supports); *publication bias / small-study effects* (explicitly present — de Vibe's higher-quality-lower-effect pattern, Goessl's small N); *argument from popularity* (rejected — popularity of mindfulness is not evidence of durability).

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8)
- **Short-term: Level 4 (High)** — that these techniques produce small-to-moderate short-term stress reduction is well supported and consensual.
- **Long-term (>6–12 mo): Level 2 (Low)** — maintenance beyond a year is largely untested; what long-term data exist are low-certainty and effects attenuate. **Not** higher, precisely because the acute effect must not be allowed to masquerade as durable relief.

---

# Limitations
- Concerns *symptom reduction*, not removal of the stressor.
- Effect sizes are modest once expectancy/active controls are accounted for.
- HRV-biofeedback's large effect rests on a small, short-follow-up base.

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **"These techniques durably relieve chronic stress."** (Steelmanned: short-term effects are real and replicated; some maintenance is seen to ~1 year and correlates with sustained practice; mechanistically, HRV/breathing engages the baroreflex/parasympathetic system.) **Rejected as a durability claim** — the maintenance data are short and low-certainty, and effects shrink under rigorous comparison. Accepted only as a *short-term* claim.
2. **"They don't work at all — it's all expectancy."** (Steelmanned: Goyal shows no advantage over active controls; much of the literature is biased.) **Rejected** — a real short-term effect survives across independent syntheses; "no better than exercise/CBT" is not "no effect."
3. **"Regular long-term practice does durably help; the trials just don't run long enough."** Plausible and **not excluded** — treated as *uncertainty* (absence of evidence), not as support. This is the honest open question.
