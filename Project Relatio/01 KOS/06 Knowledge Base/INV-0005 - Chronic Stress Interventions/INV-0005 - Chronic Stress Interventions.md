# 1. Research Question

**Original (RQ-0005, coordinator-refined):** What are the most effective intervention strategies and practices for achieving **long-term (durable) relief** from **chronic stress** in adults? Assess comparative effectiveness and, especially, the **durability** of effects.

**Refined (Specialist):** Among the intervention classes with a real evidence base — mindfulness/relaxation, slow breathing / HRV biofeedback, physical exercise, cognitive-behavioural skills (including CBT for insomnia), social connection, nature exposure, and problem-focused coping / stressor reduction — **which have evidence of durable (>6–12 month) relief from chronic stress in general adult populations, graded separately for short-term versus long-term effect?** The comparison is deliberately organised around a distinction the literature forces: interventions that **regulate the stress response** versus interventions that **reduce the stressor itself**.

*Rationale for the refinement.* The original is answerable but under-specified in the way that matters most: "effective" collapses acute and durable effects, and the mind-body literature is full of strong *acute* effects that do not obviously persist. The refinement does **not** narrow *what* is asked (all named classes are retained); it sharpens the durability axis the question explicitly prioritises and adds the response-vs-stressor distinction, which is where the honest answer lives. This is disambiguation and structuring, not material redirection (ROLE-0002 §4.1); it was not escalated.

# 2. Scope & Disambiguation

- **"Chronic stress"** here = sustained psychological/physiological stress in general adult populations (perceived stress, work stress, life stress), **not** a specific clinical disorder. Overlap with burnout, anxiety, depression, and insomnia is noted where the evidence base is shared, but the target outcome is stress reduction, not treatment of a diagnosed disorder.
- **"Durable / long-term"** = maintenance of effect at **≥6 months, and preferably ≥12 months**, after the intervention ends. Effects measured only at end-of-treatment or a few weeks later are graded as **short-term**.
- **In scope:** the intervention classes named above, assessed via systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and major RCTs.
- **Out of scope / bracketed:** individualized medical or psychological advice (this is an educational evidence review, not clinical guidance); pharmacotherapy for stress; the individual case. Where chronic stress shades into persistent burnout, clinical depression/anxiety, or trauma, the record notes that **professional help is warranted** and does not attempt to substitute for it.
- **Populations:** general and occupational adult samples. Much of the highest-volume durability evidence comes from **healthcare-worker** occupational-stress trials (the two Cochrane reviews); generalisation to other populations is a stated limitation.

# 4. Findings / Synthesis

See **FND-0005**. In brief:

1. **Response-regulation techniques** (MBSR/mindfulness, relaxation, slow breathing / HRV biofeedback, yoga/tai chi) reliably reduce stress symptoms in the **short-to-medium term (weeks to ~1 year)**, but robust **>1-year durability evidence is thin**, effects attenuate over time, and higher-quality / active-control comparisons shrink them substantially (CLM-0013).
2. **Exercise** reduces stress and distress and improves stress-response physiology, and — uniquely — carries large, durable **collateral health benefits**; it is a strong foundation even though stress-specific long-term RCT evidence is only moderate (CLM-0014).
3. **Cognitive-behavioural skills** are the best-evidenced *durable* "skills" route, with **CBT for insomnia (CBT-I)** showing genuine maintenance at 12 months and beyond; generic CBT-for-work-stress durability is weaker and mixed (CLM-0015).
4. **Social connection** is one of the strongest correlates of health and longevity and buffers stress, but the strongest evidence is **observational**; durable relief from *deliberately increasing* connection is less well established (CLM-0016).
5. **Reducing or modifying the actual stressor** (problem-focused coping for controllable stressors; organizational/work redesign) is, in principle, central to durable relief because it removes the driver rather than only damping the response — **but the interventional evidence base for stressor reduction is itself limited and mixed** (CLM-0017).
6. **Nature exposure** reliably lowers *acute* stress markers but has little evidence of *durable* chronic-stress relief (CLM-0018).

**Bottom line:** No single intervention has strong (>1 year) durability evidence for chronic-stress relief. The most defensible durable strategy is a **combination** — lifestyle foundations (sleep, exercise, connection) plus response-regulation skills plus, where the stressor is controllable, **acting on the stressor itself** — not any single technique.

# 6. Assumptions & Bracketing (KOS-0003 §10)

- **Motivated-reasoning guard (named explicitly, per instruction).** The owner of this vault runs a wellness business centred on nervous-system regulation, breathwork, and mindful movement ("stop striving, start aligning"). That is a direct **confirmation-bias hazard**: a finding that "regulation techniques durably relieve stress" would be commercially convenient. This investigation was conducted to test that proposition against the evidence, and the evidence does **not** fully support it: the response-regulation techniques closest to the business framing (mindfulness, breathwork) have the **thinnest long-term durability evidence and the largest active-control shrinkage** (CLM-0013, via Goyal 2014). The claim that most cuts against the convenient framing — that durable relief often requires reducing the stressor, not only regulating the response (CLM-0017) — is retained and argued, **and** is itself held at modest confidence because the stressor-reduction *intervention* evidence is also limited. The evidence was allowed to land where it landed in both directions.
- **Methodological:** much durability evidence is graded *low-certainty* by its own authors (both Cochrane reviews); "up to a year" is often the ceiling of available follow-up, not a demonstrated plateau. Absence of long-term evidence is treated as **uncertainty**, not as evidence of absence.
- **Publication bias / small-study effects** are pervasive in the mindfulness and mind-body literature and are named on CLM-0013 (higher-quality studies report lower effects; two-thirds of MBSR studies at considerable risk of bias).
- **Population:** heavy reliance on occupational (healthcare-worker) samples for durability; generalisation is a limitation.
- **Verification:** several effect-size figures were read from meta-analysis abstracts / search summaries, not full texts. Each is flagged on its SRC record and graded accordingly (KOS-0003 §12.1). No figure was invented.
- **Bracketed as out of scope:** the individual case; clinical treatment of diagnosed disorders; pharmacology; the metaphysics or mechanism of any specific practice beyond what allostatic-load physiology (ENT-0003) supports.
