# CLM-0017

# Reducing the Stressor Is Central to Durable Relief but Intervention Evidence Is Limited

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> When a chronic stressor is **controllable**, reducing or modifying the stressor itself — via problem-focused coping and, at the organisational level, work/schedule/workload redesign — is **in principle central to durable relief**, because it removes the driver of allostatic load rather than only damping the response; **however, the direct interventional evidence that stressor-reduction programmes produce durable relief is itself limited and mixed**, so this is asserted as a well-motivated principle rather than a strongly-proven empirical result.

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Causal** (removing the stressor produces more durable relief than only regulating the response) with a **normative-adjacent** implication (where controllable, act on the stressor). Deliberately held at modest confidence in *both* directions.

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Empirical + theoretical.**
- **SRC-0022 (coping theory):** problem-focused coping is the more adaptive **long-term** strategy for **controllable** stressors; coping-controllability *matching* predicts better adjustment; avoidance/emotion-focused coping in high-control situations predicts worse mood.
- **SRC-0017 (Cochrane, Ruotsalainen et al. 2015):** changing **work schedules** may reduce stress (low-quality); **most other organisational interventions show no clear effect** — the trial base is thin and mixed.
- **SRC-0016 (Cochrane individual):** the well-studied, effective routes are *individual* techniques — revealing the paradox: the conceptually central lever (stressor reduction) is the **less-studied** one.
- **Mechanistic (ENT-0003):** allostatic load re-accumulates if the stressor persists; only lowering the input, or durably raising buffering capacity, reduces cumulative load.

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- **Reliability: 3** — the *principle* rests on strong theory and a mechanistic rationale; the *interventional* evidence is low-certainty and mixed.
- **Relevance: 5** — directly addresses the response-vs-stressor distinction at the heart of RQ-0005.
- **Independence: 3** — coping theory and organizational trials are somewhat separate literatures; convergence is conceptual more than empirical.
- **Quality: 3** — coping-outcome evidence is largely correlational; organizational-trial evidence is sparse.
- **Limitations:** Does **not** establish that stressor-reduction programmes reliably outperform response-regulation in head-to-head durable-outcome RCTs. Controllability is subjectively appraised; many real stressors are only partly controllable.

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- Evidence strength: **Moderate** (principle), **Low** (interventional proof).
- Consensus strength: **Moderate-High** conceptually (the transactional model is foundational); **Low** on organizational-intervention efficacy.
- Relationship: strong theory, thin trials — the honest tension of this claim.

---

# Source Evaluation
SRC-0022 is authoritative for theory, weaker for outcome proof. SRC-0017 is high-authority (Cochrane); its citation has been **corrected to Ruotsalainen et al. (2015)** per Critical Review – RQ-0005 §3 (the prior PMC6494543/CD013014 is the protocol only; exact DOI still to confirm), and it reports a near-null result. The claim is therefore built on convergent reasoning, not a single decisive trial — and says so.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Ontological:** a distinction between "the stressor" and "the stress response" is real and separable (mostly true; they interact).
- **Methodological:** the scarcity of good organizational-intervention trials reflects difficulty of study design, **not** absence of effect (absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence).
- **Cultural/commercial:** this is the claim that **cuts against the owner's business framing** (which centres on regulating the response). It is retained precisely because it is inconvenient — and it is *not* inflated: its interventional evidence is honestly graded Low.

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Abductive + deductive.** *Deductively*, if allostatic load accumulates from a persistent input, then durable relief requires reducing the input or durably raising buffering — regulating the response alone leaves the input intact (ENT-0003). *Abductively*, problem-focused coping's advantage for controllable stressors is the best explanation of the coping-matching data. **Risks checked:** *motivated reasoning in the opposite direction* (the reviewer should watch that I do not over-claim stressor-reduction merely to counter the owner's bias — guarded by grading its interventional evidence Low); *false dichotomy* (response vs stressor is a spectrum, not a binary — stated); *category error* (organizational-trial nulls may reflect implementation failure, not principle failure — flagged).

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8)
- **The principle (where controllable, reducing the stressor is central to durable relief): Level 3 (Moderate)** — strong theory + mechanism, modest direct proof.
- **The interventional claim (stressor-reduction *programmes* durably outperform response-regulation): Level 2 (Low)** — the trials are few, mixed, and low-certainty.

---

# Limitations
- Many chronic stressors (bereavement, illness, structural/economic conditions) are **not** controllable by the individual — for these, response-regulation and acceptance-based coping are the appropriate levers, and this claim does not apply.
- Organizational-change evidence is weak and context-dependent.

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **"Durable relief comes mainly from regulation techniques; you don't need to change your life."** (The commercially convenient view. Steelmanned: techniques are accessible, scalable, and show short-term effects; some stressors are uncontrollable.) **Rejected as a general durability claim** — it leaves a persistent controllable stressor (and its allostatic load) intact; CLM-0013 shows the techniques' own durability is thin.
2. **"Only structural/stressor change works; techniques are a distraction."** (Steelmanned: removes the driver.) **Rejected as too strong** — the organizational-trial evidence is weak, many stressors are uncontrollable, and response-regulation has real short-term value. The evidence does not support this maximalist reading either.
3. **"It depends on controllability."** **Accepted** — this is the calibrated position: match the lever to the stressor's controllability (SRC-0022).
