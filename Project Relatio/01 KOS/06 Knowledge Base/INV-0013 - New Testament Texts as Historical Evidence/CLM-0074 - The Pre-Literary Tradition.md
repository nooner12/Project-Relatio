# CLM-0074

# The Written Sources Embed Earlier Oral and Creedal Material Datable Before the Documents Themselves — Flagship, the Pre-Pauline Creed of 1 Corinthians 15:3–7, Which the Movement Was Already Proclaiming (That Jesus Was "Raised") Within a Few Years of the Crucifixion (c. 30s CE) — a Descriptive Attestation Claim Held at HIGH Confidence, With the Resurrection AS EVENT Strictly Bracketed

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> The New Testament documents **embed earlier oral and creedal material** that predates the documents in which it appears — pushing the *attested proclamation* of the movement back toward its origin. The **flagship case** is the pre-Pauline creed of **1 Corinthians 15:3–7**, which Paul explicitly says he "received" and "passed on": its formulaic non-Pauline vocabulary, its transmission language, and its Semitic features mark it as a formula Paul inherited, datable to **within a few years of the crucifixion (c. 30s CE)** — so the movement was **already proclaiming Jesus as "raised"** that early. Other embedded material points the same way: pre-Markan passion tradition, and early hymns/formulae such as **Philippians 2:6–11** and **Romans 1:3–4**. The claim graded here is **descriptive**: *that* this early tradition is embedded and *that* the resurrection was proclaimed early — held at **High confidence**. The **truth of the resurrection as an event is strictly bracketed** (a metaphysical claim, KOS-0003 §3.6): the strong, gradable datum is *early proclamation*, not *event*.

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Descriptive** — a claim about what tradition is embedded in the sources and how early it was proclaimed; **not** a claim about the occurrence of any supernatural event.

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Historical** (source-critical analysis of embedded tradition).
- **SRC-0103 (primary texts):** 1 Cor 15:3–7 with its "received/delivered" (*paradosis*) language; Phil 2:6–11 and Rom 1:3–4 as pre-Pauline formulae.
- **SRC-0102 (Lüdemann):** the source-critical dating of the 1 Cor 15 formula to c. 30–33 CE — significant because Lüdemann is a **non-Christian** scholar who dates the *proclamation* early while denying the *event*, cleanly separating the two.
- **SRC-0097 (Sanders):** the Gospels and Paul rest on earlier tradition; the source-critical method for identifying it.

**Dual-coding (prose only):** evidence class **H-a** — the tradition is **embedded in the H-a contemporaneous Pauline text** (Paul writing c. 50s CE and himself quoting still-earlier material). This is the strongest available band, and the confidence tracks it — for the descriptive claim (early proclamation), which is what is graded.

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- Reliability: 5 — the pre-Pauline character of 1 Cor 15:3–7 is broadly accepted across the spectrum (including non-Christian scholars).
- Relevance: 5 — directly answers whether the sources reach back before the documents.
- Independence: 4 — corroborated across confessional lines (Lüdemann, Wedderburn non-Christian; Wright, Hengel confessional) — the convergence is the strength.
- Quality: 5 — the transmission language and formulaic features are objective textual markers.
- Limitations: establishes the early *proclamation*; establishes **nothing** about whether the proclaimed event occurred (bracketed).

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- Evidence strength: **High** (for the descriptive claim — embedded, early-proclaimed tradition).
- Consensus strength: **High** — that 1 Cor 15:3–7 is a pre-Pauline creed of very early date is one of the more broadly agreed source-critical results, held across confessional and non-confessional scholars. (The exact *year* is less agreed than the *fact* of early pre-Pauline origin.) Recorded separately per §9.

---

# Source Evaluation
The decisive move is the cross-confessional convergence: a non-Christian scholar (Lüdemann, SRC-0102) dates the *proclamation* as early as confessional scholars do, precisely because the dating is a source-critical result independent of belief in the event. Sanders (SRC-0097) supplies the general method; Ehrman (SRC-0100) treats the pre-literary tradition as part of the historicity argument.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Methodological:** that "received/delivered" language + non-Pauline vocabulary + Semitic features reliably mark inherited pre-Pauline formulae (mainstream).
- **Epistemological (load-bearing):** that "the movement proclaimed X early" and "X occurred" are **distinct claims** — this claim grades only the first; the second is bracketed as metaphysical.

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Inductive.** From the textual markers of embedded formulae to high confidence that early tradition is embedded and was proclaimed within a few years. **Risks checked:** *sliding from proclamation to event* (named — the single most important guard here; "the sources proclaim him raised very early" is descriptive and High; "he was raised" is metaphysical and bracketed — the confidence attaches only to the former); *apologetic over-reach* (named — the early creed is sometimes deployed as evidence *for the resurrection*; that inference is outside the historian's tools and is not made here); *over-precision on the year* (named — c. 30s, not a fixed date).

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8)
- **Level 4 (High)** — that the sources embed earlier oral/creedal tradition, and that the resurrection was **proclaimed** within a few years of the crucifixion (the 1 Cor 15 creed). **Not Level 5:** reserved; and the precise dating and the exact reconstruction of the earliest formula carry minor residual uncertainty. **The resurrection as an event is not graded at any level** — it is bracketed (KOS-0003 §3.6).

---

# Limitations
- Grades *early proclamation and embedded tradition*, nothing about the occurrence of any event.
- The precise date (c. 30s) is a reconstruction; the *fact* of pre-Pauline origin is firmer than the exact year.
- Pre-Markan passion tradition is a weaker, more inferential case than the 1 Cor 15 creed, and is held more cautiously.

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **Apologetic ("the early creed evidences the resurrection").** Rejected as a category error — the early *proclamation* is High confidence; the *event* is a metaphysical claim outside the historian's tools and is bracketed, not graded.
2. **Mythicist ("the creed refers to a celestial, non-historical Christ" — Carrier).** Steelmanned and rejected as the majority view — the "received/delivered" tradition and Paul's acquaintance with James and Peter tie the proclamation to a recently-executed figure on the majority reading; but noting it keeps the claim scoped to *proclamation*, not to independent reconstruction of the figure (deferred to INV-0014).
