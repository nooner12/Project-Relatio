# CLM-0071

# The Gospels Are Formally Anonymous (an H-a Internal Fact Held at HIGH Confidence); the Traditional Apostolic Attributions — Matthew, Mark, Luke, John — Rest on Later Second-Century Tradition Attributing Backward (H-d: Papias via Eusebius, Irenaeus) and Hold LOW Confidence: the Mirror Showcase Where Evidence-Kind and Confidence Diverge Within a Single Claim

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> The four canonical Gospels are **formally anonymous**: none names its author within the text. That the texts are anonymous is an **internal, documentary fact (H-a)** held at **High confidence**. The familiar attributions — "according to Matthew / Mark / Luke / John" — are **not internal**; they are **second-century traditions attributing the texts backward to apostolic figures** (H-d: Papias, quoted by Eusebius, on a "Mark" and a "Matthew"; Irenaeus, c. 180 CE, naming all four). Whether the traditionally-named individuals actually wrote them holds **Low confidence**: the attributions are late, dependent, and in tension with internal features (e.g. Greek composition, dependence on written sources, the third-person framing). This single claim therefore carries **two evidence classes at opposite confidences** — the anonymity (H-a, High) and the traditional authorship (H-d, Low) — the mirror-image of the manuscript case and the clearest authorship-side divergence.

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Descriptive** — a claim about authorship attestation, holding the internal fact and the traditional attribution apart.

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Historical / documentary** (internal evidence + patristic tradition).
- **SRC-0103 (primary texts):** the Gospels contain no internal self-naming; the titles ("Kata Matthaion," etc.) are superscriptions, not authorial claims within the narrative.
- **SRC-0093 (Ehrman), SRC-0094 (Brown):** the mainstream position — the Gospels are anonymous; the names are 2nd-century attributions (Brown, a Catholic scholar, concurs — a cross-confessional check).
- **SRC-0096 (Ehrman, *Forgery*):** the broader dynamics of attributing texts to authoritative figures.
- The tradition itself: **Papias** (early 2nd c., preserved in Eusebius, *Hist. eccl.* 3.39) on a "Mark" and a Hebrew "Matthew"; **Irenaeus** (*Adv. Haer.* 3.1.1, c. 180 CE) naming the four.

**Dual-coding (prose only):** the **anonymity** is **H-a** (internal documentary fact) at **High**; the **traditional authorship** is **H-d** (later tradition attributing backward) at **Low**. Within one claim, the two evidence classes carry opposite confidences. *(Axis characterization corrected per Critical Review – RQ-0013 F-1: this is a **collinear** pattern — the band tracks confidence, H-a→High and H-d→Low — not a band-vs-confidence divergence. Its value is **diagnostic**: the band explains *why* the attribution confidence must be discounted. The one genuine band-vs-confidence divergence in the investigation is CLM-0070.)*

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- Reliability: 5 (anonymity) / 2 (traditional authorship) — the texts' silence is certain; the attributions are late and dependent.
- Relevance: 5 — directly answers the "who wrote it" layer (§2.3).
- Independence: 4 (anonymity) / 2 (attribution) — the attributions trace substantially to a small 2nd-century stream (Papias→Eusebius; Irenaeus), not to independent lines.
- Quality: 5 (anonymity) / 2 (attribution) — the internal fact is documentary; the attributions are traditional and contestable.
- Limitations: establishes anonymity and the lateness of attribution; does **not** prove the named individuals did *not* write them — only that the attribution is not securely evidenced.

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- **Anonymity:** Evidence High; Consensus **High** — the formal anonymity of the Gospels is not seriously contested in critical scholarship.
- **Traditional authorship:** Evidence Low; Consensus **Contested-to-skeptical** — the critical majority doubts the traditional attributions (especially for Matthew and John); a conservative minority defends them. Recorded separately per §9.

---

# Source Evaluation
Ehrman (SRC-0093, SRC-0096) and Brown (SRC-0094) — again different confessional backgrounds, convergent on anonymity and late attribution — carry the claim; SRC-0103 supplies the internal fact. The convergence blocks a "skeptical bias" objection.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Methodological:** that superscriptions ("according to…") are editorial/traditional rather than authorial self-identification (mainstream).
- **Epistemological:** that a 2nd-century attribution is weaker evidence of authorship than internal self-identification would be (the disciplined default; the point of contention with maximalists).

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Inductive/abductive.** From textual silence + the lateness and dependence of the attributions to Low confidence in traditional authorship. **Risks checked:** *conflating anonymity with forgery* (named — anonymous is not pseudonymous; the Gospels do not falsely self-attribute, unlike the Pastorals — CLM-0072); *over-skepticism* (named — the claim does not assert the named figures certainly did *not* write them, only that the attribution is not securely evidenced; a traditional author remains possible, especially for Mark/Luke); *maximalist defense steelmanned* (the case that stable early attribution reflects genuine memory is real but rests on the same late tradition it seeks to authenticate).

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8)
**Split, by construction:**
- **Anonymity of the Gospels: Level 4 (High)** — an internal documentary fact.
- **Traditional apostolic authorship (that the named figures wrote them): Level 2 (Low)** — late, dependent, internally strained attribution.
The native rating carried in the confidence summary is **Level 4 (High) that the Gospels are anonymous / Level 2 (Low) that the traditional attributions are correct** — the divergence within one claim is the deliverable.

---

# Limitations
- Establishes anonymity and the H-d character of the attributions; does not settle *who* wrote them (largely undetermined).
- Mark and Luke attributions (non-apostolic figures) are marginally less improbable than Matthew and John (apostles), but all four rest on the same late tradition.

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **Traditional authorship largely holds (maximalist).** Steelmanned: early and fairly stable attribution could preserve genuine memory. **Rejected as high-confidence** — the attribution is 2nd-century and dependent; it cannot bear High confidence, though it keeps traditional authorship *possible* (hence Low, not Very Low/negative).
2. **The attributions are wholly fictional retrojection.** Also not established at high confidence — the claim brackets the positive question of authorship as largely undetermined; only the *attribution's evidential weakness* is asserted.
