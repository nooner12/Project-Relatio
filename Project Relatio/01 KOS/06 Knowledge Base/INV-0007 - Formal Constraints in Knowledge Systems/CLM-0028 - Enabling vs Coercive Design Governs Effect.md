# CLM-0028

# Whether Formalization Helps or Harms Is Governed Primarily by Design Orientation (Enabling vs Coercive), Not by Amount

## Draft Claim Record

---

# Claim
> The effect of a formal constraint on the people it governs is determined **less by how much formalization there is than by how it is designed**: **enabling** formalization (repairable, internally and globally transparent, flexible) supports competent work, whereas **coercive** formalization (rigid, opaque, discretion-stripping) alienates and invites evasion. This reconciles the contradictory verdicts on bureaucracy.

---

# Claim Type (KOS-0003 §3)
**Interpretive** (a design typology that reframes the question) with a **causal** component (design features → user attitudes/behaviour).

---

# Evidence (KOS-0003 §4)
Type: **Philosophical/conceptual** (framework argument) with **empirical** support accumulated in later studies.
- **SRC-0035 (Adler & Borys 1996):** proposes the enabling/coercive typology and the four distinguishing design features (repair, internal transparency, global transparency, flexibility); argues the same degree of formalization can be experienced either way.
- Subsequent management-control/accounting field studies (widely reported; not separately catalogued here) provide empirical corroboration that enabling-designed controls are experienced more positively and evaded less.

---

# Evidence Evaluation (KOS-0003 §5, 0–5)
- **Reliability: 4** — a seminal, heavily-cited framework in a top journal.
- **Relevance: 5** — directly answers "what makes a formal constraint valuable vs harmful."
- **Independence: 2** — the anchor catalogued here is a single (conceptual) source; corroborating empirical studies exist but are not catalogued in this record.
- **Quality: 3** — high as theory; but the 1996 paper is *conceptual*, not an empirical test, so the causal claim rests on later work I have not individually verified.
- **Limitations:** Establishes a **design lens**, not a measured effect size or a decision rule for optimal formalization; the four features are qualitative.

---

# Consensus (KOS-0003 §9)
- Evidence strength: **Moderate** (strong framework; empirical base largely in follow-on literature).
- Consensus strength: **High** within organizational design — the enabling/coercive distinction is a standard reference point.
- Relationship: well-accepted lens; the caveat is that it is a typology, not a quantified law.

---

# Source Evaluation
SRC-0035: very high authority and transparency, academic, inform-intent. The four design features were verified this session via converging scholarly summaries (the ASQ full-text PDF was located but not machine-readable this session) — a mild source-fidelity caveat recorded.

---

# Assumptions (KOS-0003 §10)
- **Methodological:** the workplace-procedure findings extend to schemas, standards, protocols, and knowledge-architecture governance — an **analogical** assumption, made explicit.
- **Ontological:** "design orientation" is a real, manipulable property of a formal structure — supported by the follow-on empirical literature.

---

# Reasoning (KOS-0003 §7)
**Abductive.** The enabling/coercive typology is the best available explanation of *why the same formalization gets opposite evaluations* — it dissolves the contradiction better than "more/less bureaucracy" accounts. **Risks checked:** *false dichotomy* (named — enabling/coercive is a continuum, most structures are mixed; stated in ENT-0005); *analogical overreach* (named — transfer beyond workplace procedures is flagged as analogy); *argument from authority* (guarded — graded down for the thin catalogued empirical base).

---

# Confidence (KOS-0003 §8)
**Level 3 (Moderate)** that design orientation, not amount, is the primary governor of a formal constraint's effect. Not higher: the catalogued anchor is a single conceptual paper, the empirical support sits in follow-on studies not individually verified here, and the transfer to knowledge-system constraints is analogical.

---

# Limitations
- A qualitative lens, not a metric; "enabling" is judged, not measured.
- Enabling/coercive is a continuum and multidimensional (a structure can be enabling on transparency, coercive on flexibility).
- Corroborating empirical studies are asserted, not individually catalogued in this record.

---

# Alternative Interpretations
1. **"Amount of formalization matters most (over-formalization is the problem per se)."** Steelmanned: red-tape research (Bozeman) treats burdensome *quantity* as the harm. Partly conceded, but Adler & Borys show quantity under-determines the effect; treated as complementary, not a refutation.
2. **"It's the culture/power context, not the design features."** Plausible; enabling design and supportive context likely interact. Refines rather than rejects the claim.
