# KOS-0003

# Epistemic Framework & Knowledge Validation System

## Version 1.4

---

# 1. Purpose

The Epistemic Framework defines how the Knowledge Operating System evaluates information, claims, evidence, reasoning, and conclusions.

Its purpose is to ensure that all knowledge within the KOS is:

- traceable,
- transparent,
- appropriately qualified,
- proportionally confident,
- open to revision.

The system does not determine truth directly.

Instead, it determines:

- what is claimed,
- what supports the claim,
- how strong the support is,
- what assumptions are required,
- what uncertainty remains.

---

# 2. The Fundamental Knowledge Pipeline

Every investigation shall follow:

```
Question

↓

Claims

↓

Assumptions

↓

Evidence

↓

Validation

↓

Interpretation

↓

Reasoning

↓

Confidence Assessment

↓

Knowledge Entry

↓

Future Revision
```

No major conclusion should bypass this pipeline.

---

# 3. Claim Classification System

All claims entering the KOS must be classified.

---

# 3.1 Descriptive Claims

Definition:

Claims about what exists, happened, or is observed.

Examples:

"Ancient Israelites practiced ritual sacrifice."

"The universe is expanding."

---

Evaluation:

- observation,
- documentation,
- measurement,
- historical evidence.

---

# 3.2 Causal Claims

Definition:

Claims that one factor produces another.

Structure:

```
A causes B
```

Examples:

"Meditation reduces stress."

"Social isolation increases health risks."

---

Evaluation:

Requires:

- mechanism,
- evidence,
- alternatives,
- confounding analysis.

---

# 3.3 Interpretive Claims

Definition:

Claims about meaning or significance.

Examples:

"Suffering can produce spiritual growth."

"The Eucharist represents divine participation."

---

Evaluation:

Requires:

- framework identification,
- assumptions,
- competing interpretations.

---

# 3.4 Predictive Claims

Definition:

Claims about future outcomes.

Examples:

"This policy will increase economic growth."

---

Evaluation:

Requires:

- model quality,
- historical performance,
- uncertainty.

---

# 3.5 Normative Claims

Definition:

Claims about what should be done.

Examples:

"Humans should pursue compassion."

---

Evaluation:

Requires:

- ethical assumptions,
- value framework,
- consequences.

---

# 3.6 Metaphysical Claims

Definition:

Claims about fundamental reality.

Examples:

"God exists."

"Consciousness is non-material."

"Reality is purely physical."

---

Evaluation:

Requires:

- philosophical analysis,
- assumptions,
- explanatory power,
- competing models.

---

# 4. Evidence Classification System

Evidence is classified by type.

---

# 4.1 Empirical Evidence

Information derived from observation or measurement.

Examples:

- experiments,
- surveys,
- measurements.

Primary domains:

- science,
- psychology,
- biology.

---

# 4.2 Historical Evidence

Information about past events.

Examples:

- documents,
- archaeology,
- artifacts,
- testimony.

---

# 4.3 Philosophical Evidence

Information derived from:

- logic,
- conceptual analysis,
- argumentation.

---

# 4.4 Experiential Evidence

Information derived from:

- subjective experience,
- consciousness,
- personal transformation.

---

# 4.5 Traditional Evidence

Information transmitted through:

- communities,
- institutions,
- cultural continuity.

Examples:

- religious traditions,
- oral histories.

---

# 4.6 Testimonial Evidence

Information based on reported experiences.

Evaluation requires:

- reliability,
- incentives,
- consistency,
- corroboration.

---

# 5. Evidence Evaluation Model

Every evidence item receives evaluation.

---

## Reliability

Question:

"How trustworthy is this evidence?"

Rating:

0-5

---

## Relevance

Question:

"How directly does this address the claim?"

Rating:

0-5

---

## Independence

Question:

"Does this provide unique support?"

Rating:

0-5

---

## Quality

Question:

"How rigorously was this produced?"

Rating:

0-5

---

## Limitations

Mandatory.

Every evidence item must state:

"What does this evidence not establish?"

# 6. Source Evaluation System

Every source receives assessment.

---

## Source Authority

Questions:

- Is the author qualified?
- Is the institution credible?
- Is methodology appropriate?

---

## Source Transparency

Questions:

- Are methods disclosed?
- Are conflicts of interest identified?

---

## Source Independence

Questions:

- Is the source dependent on another source?

---

## Source Intent

Questions:

- Inform?
- Persuade?
- Convert?
- Sell?
- Entertain?

Intent does not automatically invalidate a source.

It informs interpretation.

---

# 7. Reasoning Evaluation

Evidence alone does not create knowledge.

Reasoning must be evaluated.

---

# Deductive Reasoning

Question:

"If premises are true, does the conclusion follow?"

---

# Inductive Reasoning

Question:

"Does evidence reasonably support the conclusion?"

---

# Abductive Reasoning

Question:

"Is this the best explanation among alternatives?"

---

# Analogical Reasoning

Question:

"Are similarities sufficient to justify comparison?"

---

# Common Reasoning Risks

The KOS actively checks for:

- false dichotomies,
- circular reasoning,
- confirmation bias,
- argument from authority,
- emotional reasoning,
- unsupported assumptions,
- category errors.

# 8. Confidence Assessment System

Every conclusion receives a confidence rating.

> **Canonical scale — hybrid (Level + label).** Confidence is rated on a single canonical scale that pairs a **numeric level (0–5)** with a **qualitative label** (*Very High → Very Low*), always written together, e.g. **"Level 4 (High)."** The number gives an at-a-glance ordinal position; the label carries the meaning and keeps the rating legible in relational, non-numeric terms. Both are stated together — neither the bare number nor the bare word alone. **KOS-0008 §8 restates this same scale**; the two are one scale, not two vocabularies. (Consistent with §12.1 "no false precision," the number is an ordinal marker, not a measurement.)

---

## Level 5 — Very High Confidence

Strong evidence; multiple independent supports; few unresolved issues.

---

## Level 4 — High Confidence

Strong support; some limitations remain.

---

## Level 3 — Moderate Confidence

Reasonable support; meaningful uncertainty; interpretation may vary.

---

## Level 2 — Low Confidence

Limited support; major unresolved issues or significant disagreement.

---

## Level 1 — Very Low Confidence

Speculative; possible but weakly supported.

---

## Level 0 — Unsupported

Insufficient justification. An Unsupported conclusion does not qualify for the permanent Knowledge Base (§12); where it rests on fabricated or unverifiable evidence it is an automatic **Blocked** verdict (§12.1; STD-0006 §7.3).

---

# 9. Consensus Evaluation

Consensus is recorded separately.

Every claim receives:

```
Evidence Strength:

Consensus Strength:

Relationship Between Them:
```

Example:

```
Evidence:
High

Consensus:
High

Interpretation:
Strongly supported.
```

# 12.1 Evidence Integrity — Prohibition on Fabrication

This is the **inviolable requirement** of the epistemic framework. Every other rule in this document assumes it.

> **No source, citation, quotation, identifier, statistic, or datum may ever be invented.**

Rules:

1. **Do not fabricate.** Sources, authors, titles, dates, DOIs, URLs, page or verse references, sample sizes, effect sizes, and numeric results must be reported only as they are actually known.
2. **Uncertainty is stated, never filled.** Where a citation or figure is not reliably known, say so explicitly and grade the evidence accordingly (KOS-0003 §5). An honest "I am not certain of this reference" is a valid research output; a plausible invention is not.
3. **No false precision.** Approximate figures are reported as approximate. A number carried to a precision the evidence does not support is a form of fabrication.
4. **Source fidelity.** A cited passage must actually support what it is said to support. Misrepresenting a source is fabrication by another route.
5. **Verifiability.** Any claim resting on a source that cannot be located or verified must have that limitation recorded on the Claim record.

Consequence:

> **Fabricated or unverifiable evidence is an automatic `Blocked` verdict under STD-0006 §7.3.** It is not a Flagged defect to be scheduled; the object may not be relied upon until resolved.

Rationale:

A knowledge architecture's entire value is that a claim remains connected to its evidence. Fabrication severs that connection while preserving its appearance — producing knowledge that *looks* traceable and is not. Every other failure in this system is recoverable by review. This one is not, because it corrupts the record that review depends on.

This requirement is **platform-independent**. It binds any researcher, human or machine, operating within Project Relatio.

---

# 13. Final Quality Standard

The KOS asks:

Before accepting a conclusion:

1. What exactly is being claimed?
2. What evidence supports it?
3. What assumptions are required?
4. What alternatives exist?
5. How strong is the reasoning?
6. How confident should we be?
7. What would change our understanding?
