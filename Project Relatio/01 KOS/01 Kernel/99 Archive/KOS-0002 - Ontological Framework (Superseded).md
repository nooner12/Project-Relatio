# KOS-0002

# Ontological Framework

## Version 0.1

---

# 1. Purpose

The Ontological Framework defines the categories of existence and reality that the Knowledge Operating System evaluates.

The KOS does not begin with a final declaration of what reality is.

Instead, it creates a framework capable of analyzing multiple ontological positions.

---

# 2. Foundational Ontological Principle

The KOS adopts the following principle:

> Reality exists independently of human interpretation, but human access to reality occurs through experience, cognition, language, and conceptual frameworks.

Therefore:

```
Reality

↓

Human Experience

↓

Interpretation

↓

Knowledge Representation
```

Each transition introduces possible limitations.

---

# 3. Reality and Models

The KOS distinguishes between:

## Reality

The totality of what exists.

---

## Ontological Models

Human attempts to describe the structure and nature of reality.

Examples:

- physicalism,
- dualism,
- idealism,
- theism,
- non-dual philosophies.

---

## Knowledge Systems

Human frameworks used to organize understanding.

Examples:

- science,
- philosophy,
- religion,
- mythology,
- mathematics.

---

# 4. Layers of Reality

The KOS recognizes multiple analytical layers.

These are not necessarily separate realities.

They are categories of investigation.

---

# Layer 1 — Physical Reality

Includes:

- matter,
- energy,
- space,
- time,
- biological systems.

Primary investigative methods:

- science,
- observation,
- measurement.

---

# Layer 2 — Biological Reality

Includes:

- living organisms,
- evolution,
- adaptation,
- biological processes.

---

# Layer 3 — Psychological Reality

Includes:

- perception,
- cognition,
- emotion,
- memory,
- identity.

---

# Layer 4 — Social Reality

Includes:

- institutions,
- cultures,
- languages,
- traditions,
- collective meaning.

---

# Layer 5 — Abstract Reality

Includes:

- mathematics,
- logic,
- concepts,
- values,
- relationships.

The KOS recognizes that different philosophical traditions debate the status of abstract entities.

---

# Layer 6 — Spiritual / Transcendent Reality

Includes claims involving:

- God,
- gods,
- ultimate reality,
- spiritual dimensions,
- transcendence.

The KOS does not assume or reject these claims.

They are evaluated according to appropriate evidence and reasoning standards.

---

# 5. Relationship Ontology

The KOS adopts the view that relationships are fundamental objects of analysis.

A relationship can exist between:

- entities,
- concepts,
- systems,
- processes,
- experiences.

Examples:

Human ↔ Society

Human ↔ Nature

Mind ↔ Body

Individual ↔ Community

Creation ↔ Creator

Knowledge ↔ Reality

# 8. Competing Ontological Models

The KOS evaluates multiple frameworks.

---

# Physicalism

Claim:

Reality is fundamentally physical.

Strengths:

- strong scientific compatibility.

Questions:

- consciousness,
- subjective experience,
- abstract entities.

---

# Dualism

Claim:

Reality includes distinct physical and non-physical dimensions.

Strengths:

- addresses mind/body distinction.

Questions:

- interaction mechanism.

---

# Idealism

Claim:

Consciousness or mind is fundamental.

Strengths:

- directly addresses subjective experience.

Questions:

- explanation of physical regularities.

---

# Theism

Claim:

Reality ultimately depends on a divine source.

Strengths:

- addresses ultimate origin and meaning.

Questions:

- nature and evidence of divine reality.

---

# Non-Dual Frameworks

Claim:

Reality is fundamentally unified.

Strengths:

- addresses interconnectedness.

Questions:

- interpretation and verification.

---

# 9. Ontological Humility Principle

The KOS maintains:

> The complexity of reality may exceed the explanatory capacity of any single human framework.

Therefore:

- models should be compared,
- assumptions should be exposed,
- conclusions should remain proportional to evidence.

---

# 10. Relationship to Religion Research

This framework allows the KOS to compare religious systems without assuming:

- religion is false,
- religion is true,
- science explains everything,
- spirituality explains everything.

Instead:

Religious systems become complex human attempts to understand:

- existence,
- meaning,
- morality,
- suffering,
- consciousness,
- ultimate reality.

---

# 11. Ontological Evaluation Questions

For any worldview, ask:

1. What does this worldview claim exists?
2. What does it claim does not exist?
3. How does it justify those claims?
4. What experiences does it explain?
5. What problems does it leave unresolved?
6. What assumptions does it require?
