ABOUT PROJECT RELATIO
A plain-language introduction
========================================================================

Status of this document: living overview — may be updated as the project
grows. Written for a reader with no coding background and no prior exposure
to the project.
Reflects the project as of: 2026-07-21.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IT IS, IN ONE BREATH
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Project Relatio is a knowledge system. It stores individual claims —
statements like "this religious tradition began around the 3rd century" —
and it refuses to treat any claim as simply true or false. Instead, every
claim is rated, conservatively and honestly, on how strong its evidence is
and how well it has been checked. Its whole purpose is to be a body of
knowledge you can trust without having to re-verify it yourself, because it
shows its work, shows its uncertainty, and never dresses up a hunch as a
fact.

The name points at the core idea: knowledge as a structured network of
meaningful *relationships*, not a flat pile of facts.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
HOW IT RATES WHAT IT "KNOWS"
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Every claim gets rated on two separate scales, because "how strong is the
evidence" and "how well have we checked it" are genuinely different
questions.

SCALE 1 — CONFIDENCE (how strong is the evidence?)
Five levels, from Very High down to Very Low. A claim backed by solid,
agreed-upon sources scores high; a claim resting on thin or hostile
evidence scores low. The system never rounds up — if the evidence is weak,
it says so plainly.

A key honesty feature: a single claim can get SPLIT ratings. One claim
might say "*where* scholars place this: Moderate confidence, but the exact
reasoning behind it: Low confidence, because that part sits behind a
paywall we couldn't read." Rather than averaging those into one fuzzy
number, it keeps them separate and honest.

SCALE 2 — RELIANCE (has it been independently verified?)
This tracks whether a claim has actually been double-checked against
original sources, versus believed on good faith. Right now almost
everything sits at the lowest tier — reasoned but not yet independently
confirmed — so nothing is marked "safe to quote in serious work" until it
is genuinely verified. That gate is always visible, so no one mistakes a
promising idea for a proven fact.

THE CORE PHILOSOPHY
The system's value is that you can trust its ratings without re-checking
them yourself. So it grades conservatively, shows its uncertainty, keeps
strength-of-evidence separate from has-it-been-verified, and treats "we
don't know this well enough yet" as a legitimate, useful answer rather than
a failure.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
HOW THE GRADING ACTUALLY PUTS NUMBERS ON THINGS
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The system uses numbers as organized judgment, not as a formula that spits
out a score. There is no equation where you plug in facts and get
"7.3 / 10." A few separate things each get rated, and a human (with the
system's help) decides the final grade.

1. THE HEADLINE CONFIDENCE — a 1-to-5 scale.
   Very Low (1), Low (2), Moderate (3), High (4), Very High (5).
   Deliberately coarse — five honest buckets beat a fake-exact decimal.

2. THE EVIDENCE IS TIERED BY TYPE, NOT COUNTED BY VOLUME.
   The system ranks *kinds* of evidence by how trustworthy that kind is:
     - Top: a review combining many rigorous studies.
     - Next: a single well-designed controlled study.
     - Lower: a small study, an observation, or expert consensus.
     - Lowest: a reasonable theory that hasn't really been tested.
   This ranks strength, not headcount. Ten weak studies don't climb the
   tiers; one strong one does.

3. A HANDFUL OF 0-TO-5 SUB-SCORES.
   When rating a claim, specific dimensions each get a 0–5: how reliable
   the sources are, how relevant they are to this exact claim, how
   independent they are (or whether they all just echo one original
   source), and overall quality. These aren't mechanically averaged into
   the grade — they're a checklist that disciplines the judgment, so the
   final confidence isn't just a gut feeling.

4. A SEPARATE VERIFICATION NUMBER.
   Independent of all the above: "has this been double-checked against
   original sources?" Kept separate on purpose.

The point of the numbers is to force honesty and consistency and to make
the reasoning visible and checkable — not to replace the reasoning with a
calculator. And a split claim is never collapsed into one averaged number,
because averaging a "5" and a "1" into "3" would hide that one half is
rock-solid and the other is weak.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
A REAL EXAMPLE: THE YIGUANDAO CLAIM
------------------------------------------------------------------------

One actual claim asks: how does scholarship classify Yiguandao (a Chinese
religious movement) — is it a "salvationist religion" or a school of
Taoism? Here is how that one was graded.

It is a split claim, and it got two grades:
  - "Where scholars place it" -> Moderate confidence (3 of 5). Every source
    found classifies it the same way (salvationist religion, not Taoism).
    Capped at Moderate, not higher, because the source pool was small and
    English-only.
  - "The exact reasoning behind that classification" -> Low confidence
    (2 of 5). The definitive article spelling out the "why" sits behind a
    paywall the system could not open and read — so it graded that piece
    Low and said so, rather than pretending.

The sub-scores that disciplined those grades: reliability solid; relevance
maxed (the sources speak directly to the question); independence marked
DOWN because one scholar appeared in two sources, so they weren't as
independent as the raw count suggested; quality fine for the "where," weak
for the "why."

The honesty guardrails that kicked in: the claim rates only how the field
classifies Yiguandao — it deliberately does NOT claim to settle what
Yiguandao "really is." It flagged its own biggest weakness out loud (the
whole picture rests on English-language sources; a body of Chinese-language
scholarship was never accessed). And its verification tier is the lowest —
reasoned, but not independently confirmed to a quotable standard.

The takeaway: even where every source agreed, the system still refused to
call it High confidence, still split off the part it couldn't verify, still
docked itself for a repeated source, and still posted a note about what it
didn't get to.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
DOES IT CARE HOW RECENT A CLAIM IS?
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Not directly — on purpose. Age isn't treated as a measure of truth, because
sometimes the old finding is the right one and the new one doesn't hold up.
But recency comes in through a side door:

1. OLD CLAIMS GET AUTOMATICALLY FLAGGED FOR RE-CHECKING. Every claim has a
   "check me again by this date." Weaker claims are scheduled sooner. The
   system doesn't rank by age, but it does resurface aging claims: "the
   world may have moved — someone should look again." Old means worth
   revisiting, not wrong.

2. WHEN A NEW CLAIM CHALLENGES AN OLD ONE, THEY FIGHT BY EVIDENCE, NOT
   SENIORITY. Suppose an older, well-supported claim is challenged by a
   newer claim with fewer studies behind it. The system bows to neither
   automatically. "More studies" is not "stronger" — a single rigorous new
   study can outweigh a pile of weaker older ones (like one credible
   eyewitness outweighing ten people repeating a rumor). So:
     - The new claim must be reviewed and graded on the same conservative
       scale; it doesn't win just for being new.
     - If its evidence is genuinely stronger, the old claim's confidence is
       LOWERED — but the old claim is never deleted; the record of what was
       held before, and why it changed, is kept.
     - If the new claim is weaker on inspection, it may be logged as a
       challenger worth watching, without overturning anything.
     - The two are never quietly averaged into a muddle; the tension is
       resolved deliberately and the reasoning recorded.

The guardrail: a routine "still looks fine" re-check can never change a
grade on its own. Any actual change goes through a real re-evaluation with
the evidence laid out — so ratings never drift on a whim.

Bottom line: recency is a reason to RE-EXAMINE, never a reason to BELIEVE.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
HOW THE SYSTEM GETS STRONGER OVER TIME
------------------------------------------------------------------------

It doesn't just hold knowledge; it's built to improve its own judgments as
more comes in.

1. CLAIMS ARE RE-EXAMINED ON A SCHEDULE, AND GRADES CAN MOVE. Nothing
   freezes at the moment it was entered; better evidence upgrades a claim,
   contradiction drops it.

2. NEW FINDINGS RIPPLE OUTWARD. Claims are linked. When a foundational
   claim is re-graded, everything resting on it is flagged to re-check too.
   One improvement propagates to everything it touches.

3. IT CAN RAISE ITS VERIFICATION LEVEL AS IT DOES MORE LEGWORK. As original
   sources are tracked down and checked, claims climb from "we believe
   this" toward "we confirmed this against the original." The ceiling on
   how trustworthy the whole system can be rises as that work gets done.

4. GAPS ARE RECORDED AS A TO-DO LIST, NOT SWEPT AWAY. Every paywalled
   article or unreadable-language source becomes a visible worklist item.
   Filling those gaps strengthens the affected grades.

5. IT GETS STRUCTURALLY SMARTER, NOT JUST FULLER. As it studies more, it
   learns whether its own methods are working. (In one recent study of a
   family of religions, a starting assumption turned out to be wrong — and
   the system caught and corrected it.) Findings can suggest improvements
   to how the system itself is organized, so it refines its own rulebook,
   not just its contents.

The guardrail on all this growth: improvements never happen carelessly. Any
real change goes through genuine re-evaluation, and the old version is
always kept on record. The system gets stronger AND stays honest — you can
always see what changed, when, and why.

Early on it's cautious and full of "we don't know this well enough yet."
That's not a flaw — it's the starting state of something designed to EARN
its confidence over time.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE VISUAL SIDE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The grading lives in a large collection of linked text records — powerful,
but hard to see. So the project builds visual tools that turn that
structure into something you can look at and navigate.

ALREADY BUILT: a browsable map of everything the system knows.
A single interactive page you open in a web browser. An expandable tree:
each investigation opens to show its claims, each claim shows its sources.
You start zoomed out and open only what you care about, or search and jump.
Each item shows, at a glance:
  - its grade, color-coded — strong and weak claims visible without reading;
  - split grades shown as two separate marks — a claim solid in one part and
    shaky in another LOOKS that way;
  - a verification badge on every claim — a constant reminder of what's been
    confirmed versus not, so no one mistakes an unchecked idea for a fact;
  - connection markers — showing when claims relate to, depend on, or
    contradict one another.

COMING NEXT: a timeline of how religions branched from one another.
Traditions laid out across time, with lines showing which grew out of
which, and what KIND of relationship each split was (a schism, a reform, a
blend of influences, or a disputed/uncertain link — each shown
differently). Only the first family is mapped so far, so it's sparse by
design — every new group studied automatically extends the same timeline.
And because every branch line must be backed by a graded claim, the
timeline can never show a connection the evidence doesn't support. You see
history and the system's confidence in each piece of it at once.

WHY THE VISUALS MATTER:
  - Relationships become obvious — seeing five claims lean on one shaky
    source, or two findings contradict, is instant in a picture.
  - Meaning comes from the shape — how knowledge clusters and connects shows
    where the system is solid, where it's thin, and where the tensions are.
  - The honesty stays in view — grades and "not yet verified" flags travel
    WITH the picture, so you're never shown a clean, confident-looking
    diagram that hides how much is still uncertain.

The visuals aren't decoration on top of the real work — they're a way to
SEE the reasoning, including its uncertainty: not just what the system
concludes, but how strongly, based on what, and how it all connects.
